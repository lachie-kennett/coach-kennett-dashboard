# Bloodwork Analysis Knowledge Base
## Coach Kennett — Functional Blood Analysis Reference

---

## PHILOSOPHY & ETHICS

### Core Principles
- Blood testing is a snapshot in time. Accuracy improves with serial tests to analyse patterns.
- Combine symptoms + clinical experience + client history in assessment. Do not overestimate blood testing alone.
- 0.012–0.6% of all labs have significant error. If a result doesn't make clinical sense, retest before acting.
- The goal is to identify optimal function, not just detect overt pathology.
- Use the approach: "Is this person functioning at their highest possible level — and if not, what's moving them away from it?"

### Ethical Boundaries
**Do NOT:**
- Present as a dietitian, doctor, or licensed medical professional
- Give medical advice or make clinical judgments
- Diagnose conditions or speak in absolutes
- Tell clients to stop, change, or adjust medications
- Use blood results to pressure or scare clients
- Base prescriptions solely on blood work

**Best Practice:**
- Present interpretations as educational
- Refer to qualified medical professionals when concerns arise
- Use evidence-based information
- Always include a disclaimer clarifying non-medical role
- Operate within functional ranges and scope of practice
- Obtain informed consent

## PRE-TEST GUIDELINES

### Timing
- Blood draw between 8–10 AM, ~12 hours from last meal
- Captures peak testosterone levels
- Be consistent with draw time across tests

### Restrictions
- No structured exercise 24 hours prior (light walking OK)
- No drugs or alcohol 72 hours prior
- Cease supplementation 3 days before (protein powder and omega-3s are OK)

### Hydration
- Drink 1–1.5 L of water before the test
- Dehydration can mask anemia and falsely elevate haemoglobin and haematocrit

### Delay Test If
- High stress events in the prior 24–48 hours (retest in 1 week)
- Abnormally poor sleep

### Females
- Get blood drawn 7 days after ovulation to measure peak luteal phase hormonal response
- Avoid during or right after menstruation unless specified by GP

## CONVENTIONAL vs OPTIMAL RANGES

### Key Concept
- "Normal" ranges = population averages including people with early metabolic dysfunction
- Optimal ranges = physiological sweet spot for peak function
- Standard intervals span 2 standard deviations (~95% of population), which includes many with early dysfunction
- Optimal ranges are narrower and detect early changes before disease progression

### Example — Blood Glucose
- Values above 85 mg/dL (4.7 mmol/L) linked to increased CVD risk even without diabetes
- Optimal fasting glucose: 75–86 mg/dL (4.2–4.8 mmol/L)
- Conventional "normal": 65–99 mg/dL (3.6–5.5 mmol/L)

## DEHYDRATION & BLOOD TESTS

### What Happens
- Dehydration decreases blood volume → blood becomes concentrated
- Causes falsely elevated haemoglobin and haematocrit
- Can mask anemia (dehydrated + anemic = hematocrit looks "normal")
- Can mimic polycythemia (falsely elevated RBCs)

### Dehydration Markers on Blood Test
- ↑ Hematocrit
- ↑ Haemoglobin
- ↑ RBC count
- ↑ Total protein
- ↑ Albumin, globulin, fibrinogen
- ↑ Sodium, potassium (relative)

### Practical
- Instruct clients to drink 1–1.5 L before blood draw

## BLOOD COMPONENTS

### Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)
- Carry O₂ from lungs to tissues, CO₂ back to lungs
- Biconcave shape = maximum surface area for gas exchange
- No nucleus = more room for haemoglobin
- ~300 million haemoglobin molecules per RBC
- ~70% of body iron is in haemoglobin

### Buffy Coat (<1% of blood)
- **Platelets**: clot formation, seal damaged vessels
- **White blood cells**: immune defence, destroy pathogens

### Plasma (~55% of blood)
- 90% water — the "highway" for all blood cells
- **Albumin**: fluid balance
- **Globulins**: carry antibodies, transport hormones/lipids
- **Fibrinogen**: scaffolding for clots
- **Electrolytes**: sodium, potassium, calcium, chloride, bicarbonate

## HAEMATOLOGY PANEL

---

### HAEMOGLOBIN (Hb)

**What it is:** Protein in RBCs that carries oxygen. 70% of body iron found in Hb.

**Use:** Assess severity of anaemia or polycythaemia. Combine with iron panel to determine TYPE of anaemia.

**Optimal Ranges:**
- Male: 14–15 g/dL (140–150 g/L)
- Female: 13.5–14.5 g/dL (135–145 g/L)

**Conventional Ranges:**
- Male: 13–17 g/dL
- Female: 11.5–15.5 g/dL

**What Influences Hb:**
- Iron levels (deficiency = low Hb)
- O₂ demand (altitude/training = ↑ Hb)
- Hormones (EPO stimulates RBC production)
- Nutrition (B12, folate essential)
- Hydration (dehydration = falsely ↑, overhydration = ↓)

**Low Hb — Anaemia:**
- Iron deficiency anaemia (most common)
- B12/folate deficiency
- B6 deficiency
- Chronic disease/inflammation
- Internal bleeding
- Copper deficiency
- Vitamin C need

**High Hb:**
- Dehydration (most common)
- Asthma/emphysema
- Polycythaemia
- High altitude

**Note:** Dehydration can mask anaemia — always consider hydration status.

---

### RED BLOOD CELL COUNT (RBC)

**What it is:** Number of RBCs per microlitre of blood.

**Optimal Ranges:**
- Male: 4.80–5.50 x10¹²/L
- Female: 4.30–4.80 x10¹²/L

**Low RBC Causes:**
- Anaemia (iron, B12/folate, B6, copper)
- Internal bleeding
- Pregnancy

**High RBC Causes:**
- Dehydration
- Hypoxia / high altitude
- Polycythaemia

**Note:** Dehydration concentrates blood — can mask anaemia and mimic polycythaemia.

---

### HAEMATOCRIT (HCT)

**What it is:** Proportion (%) of RBCs in a volume of blood.

**Optimal Ranges:**
- Male: 40–48% (0.40–0.48 L/L)
- Female: 37–44% (0.37–0.44 L/L)

**Always assess with:** Hb, RBC, MCV, MCH, ferritin, serum iron, TIBC

**Low HCT causes:**
- Iron/B12/folate/B6/copper deficiency anaemia
- Digestive inflammation
- Vitamin C deficiency
- Internal bleeding
- Pregnancy

**High HCT causes:**
- Dehydration
- Polycythaemia
- Asthma/emphysema
- Anabolic steroids

---

### MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume)

**What it is:** Average SIZE of RBCs (cubic microns).

**Classification:**
- <82 fL = Microcytic (small cells — iron/B6 deficiency)
- 82–89.9 fL = Normocytic (normal)
- >89.9 fL = Macrocytic (large cells — B12/folate deficiency)

**Optimal Range:** 82–89.9 fL

**Low MCV (Microcytic):**
- Iron deficiency
- B6 deficiency
- Copper deficiency
- Thalassaemia
- Chronic disease/inflammation

**High MCV (Macrocytic):**
- B12 or folate deficiency
- Vitamin C deficiency
- Hypothyroidism
- Hypochlorhydria
- Liver disease/alcohol

**Important:** MCV can appear NORMAL if client has BOTH B12/folate AND iron deficiency simultaneously. Use RDW to unmask this.

**If oral B12 not working:** High MCV = macrocytic RBCs → malabsorption likely → B12 injections may be required.

---

### MCH (Mean Corpuscular Haemoglobin)

**What it is:** Average WEIGHT of haemoglobin per RBC (calculated, not directly measured).

**Optimal Range:** 28–31.9 pg

**Low MCH:**
- Iron or copper deficiency
- B6 deficiency (if iron panel looks normal)
- Heavy metal toxicity

**High MCH:**
- B12 or folate deficiency
- Hypochlorhydria

---

### MCHC (Mean Corpuscular Haemoglobin Concentration)

**What it is:** Average haemoglobin CONCENTRATION in packed RBCs (calculated).

**Optimal Range:** 34–36 g/dL
(Note: No MCHC occurs over 37 g/dL — physical limit of haemoglobin in RBCs)

**Low MCHC:**
- Iron deficiency
- B6 deficiency
- Vitamin C deficiency
- Heavy metal toxicity (lead, cadmium)
- Heavy periods / thalassaemia

**High MCHC:**
- B12 or folate deficiency
- Hypochlorhydria

---

### RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width)

**What it is:** Variation in RBC SIZE (%). Use alongside MCV — without RDW, MCV interpretation is incomplete.

**Optimal Range:** 11–12.6%

**Critical insight:** RDW tells you what's been happening over the LAST 3–4 MONTHS. A "normal" MCV can be misleading if you have mixed small and large cells averaging out. High RDW unmasks this.

**High RDW (>12.6%):**
- B12 and/or folate deficiency
- Iron deficiency
- Mixed deficiencies (most likely cause of very high RDW)
- Signals nutritional deficiency for the past 3–4 months

**Low RDW:**
- Uniform RBC size (ideal)
- More consistent with iron or copper deficiency (fewer size variations)

## WHITE BLOOD CELL PANEL

---

### WBC (White Blood Cell Count)

**What it is:** Total leukocytes in blood. General marker of immune activity.

**Optimal Range:** 3.8–6.0 x10⁹/L
**Conventional:** 3.8–10.8 x10⁹/L

**Low WBC:**
- Chronic viral/bacterial infection
- Immunosuppression
- Bone marrow disorders
- Poor protein intake
- High-output athletes

**High WBC:**
- Acute bacterial/viral infection
- Stress (cortisol → ↑ WBC)
- Allergies
- Post-exercise
- Refined diet

---

### NEUTROPHILS

**What they are:** First responders; most common WBC (should be ~50%+ of WBC). Fight bacteria via phagocytosis.

**Optimal (Absolute):** 1.9–4.2 x10⁹/L

**Low Neutrophils (Neutropenia):**
- Chronic viral infection
- Chronic inflammation
- Intestinal permeability
- Low zinc

**High Neutrophils (Neutrophilia):**
- Acute/localised bacterial infection (primary cause)
- Stress
- Childhood diseases
- Inflammation

**Note:** Elevated in bacterial infections; decreased in chronic viral infections.

---

### LYMPHOCYTES

**What they are:** B cells (produce antibodies) and T cells (attack infected cells).

**Optimal (Absolute):** 1.44–2.54 x10⁹/L

**Neutrophil:Lymphocyte Ratio (NLR):**
- Healthy: ~2:1 (neutrophils:lymphocytes)
- Higher NLR: bacterial infection/stress/inflammation
- Lower NLR: viral infection
- NLR >10: severe bacterial infection/sepsis
- NLR ~1:1: autoimmunity or immune dysregulation

**Low Lymphocytes:**
- Chronic viral/bacterial infection
- Chronic stress (cortisol suppresses lymphocytes)
- Bone marrow suppression
- Oxidative stress

**High Lymphocytes:**
- Acute/chronic viral infection
- Infectious mononucleosis (EBV)
- Chronic infection
- Autoimmune inflammation
- Systemic toxicity/poor detox

---

### MONOCYTES

**What they are:** Largest WBC. Second line of defence. Clean up dead cells, microorganisms, debris. Rise during RECOVERY phase of infection.

**Optimal (Absolute):** 0.20–0.40 x10⁹/L

**High Monocytes:**
- Recovery from acute infection (positive sign)
- Intestinal parasites (with ↑ eosinophils + ↑ basophils)
- Liver dysfunction
- BPH in males (↑ monocytes + ↑ creatinine in men >40)

**Fasting note:** Fasting lowers monocyte count and reduces inflammatory activity — useful strategy for clients with chronically elevated monocytes.

---

### EOSINOPHILS

**What they are:** Fight parasites and respond to allergies/sensitivities. Not effective against bacteria.

**Optimal (Absolute):** 0.03–0.20 x10⁹/L

**High Eosinophils:**
- Intestinal parasites (especially if eosinophils >3% + basophils >1% + monocytes >7%)
- Food/environmental allergy or sensitivity (eosinophils >3%)
- Asthma

**Note:** Diurnal rhythm — lowest in morning, rises midday to midnight.

---

### BASOPHILS

**What they are:** <1% of WBC. Release histamine and heparin. Fight parasites. Chronic elevation = chronic histamine reaction.

**Optimal (Absolute):** 0.00–0.10 x10⁹/L

**High Basophils:**
- MCAS (mast cell activation syndrome)
- Asthma / mould sensitivity / allergies
- Non-specific inflammation (bursitis, tendinitis, fibromyalgia)
- Intestinal parasites (with ↑ eosinophils + ↑ monocytes)

---

### PLATELETS

**What they are:** Clot formation — the "glue" that stops bleeding. Produced from megakaryocytes in bone marrow. Lifespan: 8–10 days.

**Optimal Range:** 190–300 x10⁹/L

**High Platelets (Thrombocytosis):**
- Iron deficiency (bone marrow shifts to platelet production)
- Atherosclerosis
- Inflammatory disorders
- Spleen removal

**Low Platelets (Thrombocytopenia):**
- Heavy metal toxicity
- Oxidative stress
- Infections
- Nutrient deficiencies (B12, folate, iron, vitamin D, K, C)
- Certain medications (ibuprofen, aspirin, acetaminophen)

**Atherosclerosis pattern:** ↑ platelets + ↑ uric acid + triglycerides > HDL + ↑ LDL + ↑ CRP + ↑ homocysteine + ↑ fibrinogen

---

### ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate)

**What it is:** Rate at which RBCs settle in a test tube. Non-specific marker of inflammation.

**Optimal Ranges:**
- Male: 0–5 mm/hr
- Female: 0–10 mm/hr

**Elevated ESR causes:**
- Tissue inflammation (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, gout, nephritis)
- Tissue destruction (carcinoma, lymphoma, autoimmune)
- Multiple myeloma / lymphoma
- Cardiovascular: myocardial infarction (NOT angina)

**Falsely Low:** High albumin, high blood sugar, steroids, aspirin
**Falsely High:** Elevated fibrinogen/globulins/cholesterol, oral contraceptives, heparin, young children

## IRON PANEL

### The Iron Boats System (Metaphor)
- **Iron** = cargo
- **Transferrin** = boats ferrying iron through blood (each carries up to 2 iron units)
- **Transferrin Saturation** = % of boats loaded with iron
- **Ferritin** = docks/warehouses storing iron safely
- Low iron stores → liver makes MORE boats (↑ transferrin) but few are loaded (↓ transferrin sat)
- High inflammation → docks absorb iron (↑ ferritin), fewer boats sent (↓ transferrin)

---

### SERUM IRON

**What it is:** Iron bound to transferrin, circulating in plasma (NOT inside RBCs).

**Key insight:** Iron panel shifts within 4–8 weeks. Haematology is a LAGGING measure (6+ months). You can have low serum iron but normal haemoglobin/HCT — the anaemia will show later.

**Optimal Range:** 85–130 µg/dL (15.22–23.27 µmol/L)

**Low Serum Iron:**
- Iron deficiency anaemia
- Hypochlorhydria (low stomach acid impairs absorption)
- Internal bleeding
- Chronic infection (sequestration)
- Hypothyroidism

**High Serum Iron:**
- Liver dysfunction
- Haemochromatosis / iron overload
- Viral infection
- Iron conversion problem (iron available but can't be incorporated — needs B12, B6, folate, copper)

**Stages of Iron Deficiency:**
1. Ferritin ↓ <20 ng/mL; serum iron + Hb normal
2. Serum iron ↓ <50 µg/dL; transferrin sat ↓ <16%; transferrin ↑
3. Anaemia with normocytic RBCs
4. Microcytosis + hypochromia (low MCV, low MCH)
5. Symptomatic: fatigue, dizziness, weakness, pallor, restless legs

**Iron Absorption:**
- Daily requirements: Males 8 mg, Females 18 mg, Pregnant 27 mg
- Only ~10% of dietary iron absorbed
- Heme iron (animal) > non-heme iron (plant)
- Vitamin C enhances non-heme iron absorption
- Absorb best 45–50 min from food
- Inhibitors: dairy, coffee, tea, high fibre, phytates, oxalates, zinc/manganese supplements
- Take iron 1 hour away from coffee/tea

---

### TRANSFERRIN

**What it is:** Liver-produced protein that transports iron. Inversely proportional to iron stores — if iron is LOW, liver makes MORE transferrin.

**Optimal Range:** 200–360 mg/dL (2.0–3.6 g/L)

**High Transferrin:**
- Iron deficiency (liver making more boats)
- Pregnancy

**Low Transferrin:**
- Iron overload / haemochromatosis
- Inflammation (negative acute-phase reactant — cytokines suppress transferrin)
- Liver disease (reduced production)
- Malnutrition

**Inflammatory Anaemia Pattern:** Low transferrin + high ferritin + low transferrin saturation = anemia of inflammation (NOT simple iron deficiency)

---

### TRANSFERRIN SATURATION

**What it is:** % of transferrin's iron-binding sites occupied by iron.
Formula: (Serum Iron ÷ TIBC) × 100

**Optimal Range:** 24–35%

**Low Saturation (<24%):**
- Iron deficiency anaemia
- Pregnancy (3rd trimester)
- Chronic infection

**High Saturation (>35%):**
- Haemochromatosis / iron overload
- Haemolytic anaemia
- B12/folate deficiency
- Thalassaemia

**Classic Iron Deficiency Pattern:** ↓ serum iron + ↑ TIBC → low transferrin saturation (lots of empty boats)

---

### FERRITIN

**What it is:** Primary STORAGE form of iron. Most sensitive early marker for iron deficiency. Also an acute-phase reactant — rises with inflammation/infection.

**Optimal Range:** 45–79 ng/mL
- 80–160: mild iron overload
- >200: possible iron overload or inflammation

**Critical note:** Ferritin ALONE is not enough. Must assess full iron panel (iron + ferritin + transferrin sat + TIBC + haematology).

**Low Ferritin (<45 ng/mL):**
- Iron deficiency (drops BEFORE serum iron drops, before anaemia develops)
- Hypoferritinaemia without anaemia: fatigue, weakness, hair loss, depression still possible
- Hypochlorhydria
- Copper deficiency
- Hypothyroidism (↑ hepcidin blocks absorption)
- Vitamin C deficiency

**High Ferritin (>79 ng/mL):**
- Haemochromatosis / iron overload
- Inflammation / infection (acute-phase response — body sequesters iron to starve pathogens)
- Liver dysfunction
- Alcohol (suppresses hepcidin → ↑ iron absorption)
- Cardiovascular risk marker
- Insulin resistance in menopause

**Female testing note:**
- Test in WEEK 3 of menstrual cycle (not week 1–2)
- After 8–10 hour fast, no supplements/alcohol/high-intensity exercise for 48–72 hrs
- Include Hb and CRP to rule out pseudo-deficiency from inflammation
- Ferritin of 30 µg/L can be adequate if transferrin 2.0–2.5 g/L and transferrin sat 20–30%

**Increasing Iron Protocol:**
1. Rule out absorption issues (hypochlorhydria, infection, heavy periods)
2. Rule out cofactor deficiencies (copper, vitamin C)
3. Adjust diet + supplementation 2–4x RDI
4. Heme > non-heme; vitamin C needed for non-heme
5. Retest at 4–6 weeks

## GLUCOSE & METABOLISM

---

### FASTING GLUCOSE

**What it is:** Baseline blood sugar after 8–12 hour fast. Regulated by insulin, cortisol, glucagon, nervous system.

**Optimal Range:** 75–86 mg/dL (4.1–4.8 mmol/L)
**Conventional:** 65–99 mg/dL (3.61–5.50 mmol/L)

**Low Fasting Glucose:**
- Reactive hypoglycaemia
- Adrenal hypofunction
- Undereating
- Liver damage

**High Fasting Glucose:**
- Insulin resistance
- B1 (thiamine) deficiency
- Stress (cortisol → gluconeogenesis)
- Poor sleep
- Metabolic syndrome
- PCOS

---

### NON-FASTING GLUCOSE

**What it is:** Blood glucose at any time, reflecting real-world glycaemic control.

**Optimal Range:** 75–125 mg/dL (4.16–6.94 mmol/L)

**Target:** 2-hour post-meal glucose <140 mg/dL; 1-hour post-meal <155 mg/dL

**Risk thresholds:**
- 2-hr PPG >140 mg/dL → suspect diabetes
- 2-hr PPG ≥200 mg/dL → diagnostic for diabetes
- Late post-meal glucose ≥105 mg/dL → ↑ CVD mortality risk

**Key insight:** Non-fasting glucose can detect insulin resistance BEFORE fasting glucose or HbA1c rises.

---

### INSULIN (FASTING)

**What it is:** Hormone from pancreatic β-cells regulating blood sugar. Promotes storage mode.

**Optimal Range:** 2–5 µIU/mL
**Conventional:** 0–18.4 µIU/mL

**Insulin Functions:**
- ↓ blood glucose (moves glucose into cells)
- Converts glucose/protein to fat
- Removes fat from blood → stores in adipose
- Stimulates hepatic cholesterol synthesis

**Low Insulin:**
- Type 1 diabetes / β-cell destruction

**High Insulin (Hyperinsulinemia):**
- Insulin resistance
- Metabolic syndrome
- Cushing's disease
- PCOS
- Obesity / chronic stress
- Linked to ↑ heart attack, stroke, diabetes risk

**Progression:** Normal glucose tolerance → reactive hypoglycaemia (hyperinsulinemia) → insulin resistance → type 2 diabetes

---

### HbA1c

**What it is:** % of haemoglobin glycosylated over the past 2–3 months (lifespan of RBC ~120 days). Rolling average of glucose control.

**Optimal Range:** 4.6–5.3%
**Conventional:** 0–5.7%

**Note:** Poor sensitivity for early diabetes detection. OGTT ± insulin is preferred for screening.

**High HbA1c:**
- Metabolic syndrome / pre-diabetes / diabetes
- Thiamine need
- Chronic stress

**Low HbA1c:**
- Hypoglycaemia
- Haemolytic anaemia
- High-dose vitamin C (can falsely ↓ or ↑)

**Nutraceutical Protocol — Glucose Regulation:**
- **Berberine:** 500 mg 3x/day → ↓ FBG, ↓ HbA1c, ↓ insulin, ↓ HOMA-IR, ↓ TC, ↓ LDL
- **Black Cumin Seed Oil:** 2g/day for 6–8 weeks → 56 mg/dL reduction in FBG
- **Herbal blend** (nettle, berry, onion, garlic, fenugreek, walnut, cinnamon): 12 weeks → ↓ fasting + postprandial glucose + HbA1c

## LIPID PANEL

---

### TOTAL CHOLESTEROL

**What it is:** Sum of all cholesterol-carrying lipoproteins. Found in every cell. Essential for cell membranes, steroid hormones, vitamin D, myelin sheaths, bile salts.

**Optimal Range:** 160–199 mg/dL (4.14–5.15 mmol/L)

**Key insight:** Total cholesterol alone has limited utility. Only useful if concerned about familial hypercholesterolaemia (FH). Must assess individual risk factors for context.

**Low Total Cholesterol:**
- Oxidative stress (sudden drop = free radical pathology)
- Liver/gallbladder dysfunction
- Low fat diet
- Thyroid hyperfunction
- Malnutrition

**High Total Cholesterol:**
- Metabolic syndrome
- Cardiovascular disease risk
- Hypothyroidism (↑ TSH + ↑ triglycerides + ↑ CHOL)
- Adrenal insufficiency
- Gallbladder dysfunction
- Fatty liver (early)
- Insulin resistance

**Note on statins:** Inhibit HMG-CoA reductase → reduce both cholesterol AND CoQ10 production → impacts mitochondrial and cardiovascular health.

---

### HDL (High-Density Lipoprotein)

**What it is:** "Good cholesterol" — reverse cholesterol transport, picks up excess cholesterol and returns it to liver. Anti-inflammatory and antioxidant.

**Optimal Range:** 55–93 mg/dL (1.42–2.40 mmol/L)

**Low HDL:**
- Atherosclerosis risk
- Metabolic syndrome / hyperinsulinemia
- Refined carbohydrate diet
- Obesity / fatty liver
- Sedentary lifestyle
- Heavy metals
- Hyperthyroidism

**High HDL:**
- Autoimmune activity (↑ HDL + ↓ triglycerides + low/normal CHOL)
- Heavy exercise
- Exogenous oestrogen

**Pattern for hyperlipidaemia:** HDL <25% of total cholesterol → hyperlipidaemia likely

---

### LDL (Low-Density Lipoprotein)

**What it is:** Carries cholesterol from liver to peripheral tissues. Higher circulating LDL = ↑ atherosclerosis risk. Independent risk factor for CVD.

**Optimal Range:** 80–99.99 mg/dL (2.07–2.59 mmol/L)

**Key insight:** Assess particle SIZE — small dense LDL is more atherogenic. Use ApoB for particle count.

**Cholesterol hyper-responders:** 15–25% of population have genes causing significant cholesterol rise with high saturated fat diets. 1/300 have extreme response.

**High LDL:**
- Metabolic syndrome / hyperinsulinemia
- Atherosclerosis / CVD risk
- Fatty liver
- Oxidative stress (LDL peroxidation promotes plaque)
- Refined carbohydrate diet

---

### NON-HDL CHOLESTEROL

**What it is:** Total Cholesterol − HDL. Represents ALL atherogenic lipoproteins (LDL + VLDL + IDL + Lp(a)).

**Optimal Range:** 70–99 mg/dL (1.81–2.56 mmol/L)

**Key insight:** Better predictor of CVD than total cholesterol or LDL alone. Captures small, dense LDL which infiltrates arterial walls.

**Note:** Lp(a) is possibly the most atherogenic particle. Lower Lp(a) = lower cardiovascular mortality risk.

---

### TRIGLYCERIDES

**What it is:** Lipids formed from unused calories (especially sugar/carbs). Stored in fat cells. Packaged in VLDL for transport. Should ≈ ½ of total cholesterol in healthy metabolism.

**Optimal Range:** 70–80 mg/dL (0.79–0.90 mmol/L)
**Conventional:** 0–149.99 mg/dL

**Low Triglycerides:**
- Liver/biliary dysfunction
- Low fat/vegetarian diet
- Thyroid hyperfunction
- Autoimmune processes

**High Triglycerides:**
- Metabolic syndrome / hyperinsulinemia
- Fatty liver
- Insulin resistance
- Obesity / alcohol / diabetes
- Hypothyroidism
- Refined carbohydrate diet

**Pattern for metabolic syndrome:** Triglycerides > total cholesterol + ↑ LDL + ↓ HDL + ↑ fasting glucose

**Pattern for atherosclerosis:** ↑ triglycerides relative to total CHOL + ↑ uric acid + ↓ HDL + ↑ LDL + ↑ platelets + ↑ homocysteine + ↑ CRP + ↑ fibrinogen

---

### APOLIPOPROTEIN B (ApoB)

**What it is:** Structural protein for VLDL/LDL. Measures NUMBER of atherogenic particles (not just cholesterol content). More direct measure of cardiovascular risk than LDL-C alone.

**Risk Categories:**
- Optimal: <90 mg/dL
- Moderate: 90–119 mg/dL
- High: ≥120 mg/dL

**High ApoB:**
- CVD risk
- Decreased LDL clearance
- High-fat diet
- Diabetes
- Metabolic syndrome
- Hypothyroidism

**Lowering ApoB:**
- Ginger 2g/day for 12 weeks → 13.39% reduction
- CoQ10 → lowers Lp(a)

## LIPID NUTRACEUTICAL PROTOCOLS

### Framework for Cholesterol/Disease Risk Assessment
1. **Protective markers:** Omega-3 index (want 8%+), CoQ10 status
2. **Inflammatory/clotting markers:** CRP <5.0 nmol/L, Fibrinogen <9.0 µmol/L, ESR, Homocysteine <10 µmol/L
3. **Metabolic:** HbA1c <5.3%, Fasting insulin <6 µIU/mL
4. **Lipoproteins:** ApoB <80 mg/dL, Lp(a)

### Protocols

**Berberine:** 500 mg 2–3x/day
- ↓ TC 29%, ↓ Triglycerides 35%, ↓ LDL 25%
- Also: ↓ fasting glucose, ↓ HbA1c, ↓ insulin
- Activates AMPK

**Citrus Bergamot:**
- 500mg: ↓ TC 20%, ↓ LDL 23%, ↑ HDL 25.9%
- 1000mg: ↓ TC 30.9%, ↓ LDL 38.6%, ↑ HDL 39%

**CoQ10 (200 mg/day):**
- 21% ↓ cholesterol, 22% ↓ LDL, 25% ↑ HDL, 37% ↓ IL-6
- Also lowers Lp(a)

**Black Cumin Seed Oil (1–2g/day):**
- ↓ triglycerides 18%, ↓ TC 13%, ↑ HDL 30%, ↓ LDL 17.8%
- Also improves liver enzymes (ALT, AST)

**Omega-3s (2–4g EPA/DHA):**
- Near-linearly lowers triglycerides and non-HDL cholesterol
- JELIS trial: 1.8g EPA → 19% ↓ cardiovascular events; 53% ↓ in high-risk subgroup
- Improves omega-3 index → may improve RBC membrane flexibility

**Soluble Fibre (3–15g/day):**
- ↓ LDL 5–15%, ↓ triglycerides, ↓ blood sugar
- Sources: black beans, chickpeas, lentils, psyllium, PHGG

**Garlic (20g raw/day):**
- ↓ TC, ↓ LDL, ↓ blood pressure
- Best combined with lemon juice

**Pomegranate Peel Extract (1500 mg/day):**
- ↓ TC 6%, ↓ triglycerides 10%, ↑ HDL 12%, ↓ LDL 10%

## CLINICAL PATTERNS QUICK REFERENCE

### Iron Deficiency Anaemia
↓ serum iron, ↓ ferritin, ↓ transferrin sat, ↑ TIBC, ↑ transferrin, ↓ MCV, ↓ MCH, ↓ MCHC, ↓ Hb, ↓ HCT, ↑ RDW

### Anaemia of Inflammation
↑ ferritin, ↓ serum iron, ↓ transferrin, ↓ transferrin sat, ↓ TIBC, normal/↑ CRP

### B12/Folate Deficiency Anaemia

## BIOCHEMISTRY PANEL — ELECTROLYTES

### Sodium (Na⁺)
- **Conventional:** 135–146 mmol/L | **Optimal:** 137–142 mmol/L
- Most abundant extracellular cation (~90% of ECF electrolytes)
- **Functions:** osmotic pressure, acid-base balance, nerve impulse conduction, blood pressure regulation
- Serum sodium reflects **water balance and hormonal regulation**, NOT dietary salt intake
- Regulated by: aldosterone, ADH/vasopressin, renin-angiotensin system (RAAS), renal blood flow
- **Key insight:** serum sodium reflects *concentration*, not total body sodium. Urinary sodium is a more sensitive marker of true sodium balance.

**Hormonal regulation — RAAS cascade:**
Low BP → kidneys release renin → angiotensinogen → angiotensin I → ACE → angiotensin II → vasoconstriction + aldosterone release → sodium retention + water follows → BP rises

**Na/K Ratio patterns:**
- ↑ sodium + ↓ potassium → adrenal stress load (high aldosterone)
- ↓ sodium + ↑ potassium → adrenal insufficiency (low aldosterone)

**Low sodium (hyponatremia) causes:** adrenal hypofunction/Addison's, edema, diuretics, diarrhea, vomiting, diabetes, SIADH
- Adrenal insufficiency pattern: ↓ Na, ↑ K, ↓ Cl, ↓ aldosterone, ↓ cortisol, ↑ urinary Cl
- Confirm with salivary cortisol

**High sodium (hypernatremia) causes:** dehydration (most common), Cushing's, adrenal hyperfunction
- Dehydration pattern: ↑ Na, ↑ HGB, ↑ HCT, ↑ RBC, ± ↑ K (acute); also ↑ albumin, ↑ BUN (chronic)
- Adrenal stress pattern: ↑ Na, ↓ K, ↓ urinary Cl

**Practical coaching:**
- Stress → cortisol + RAAS activation → sodium/water retention → scale weight jumps 1–2kg overnight (not fat)
- Low-carb dieting → ↓ insulin → sodium dump → "first-week weight loss" + electrolyte depletion risk

**Drugs that may decrease sodium:** heparin, laxatives, sulfates, diuretics
**Drugs that may increase sodium:** steroids, aspirin, NSAIDs, antihypertensives

---

### Potassium (K⁺)
- **Conventional:** 3.5–5.3 mmol/L | **Optimal:** 4.0–5.0 mmol/L
- ~90% is intracellular; serum concentration can be 15–20× lower than intracellular
- **Primary intracellular cation** — main intracellular pH buffer
- **Functions:** nerve impulse conduction, muscle contraction, osmotic pressure, cardiac rhythm regulation, renal pH balance
- Regulated by aldosterone (increases urinary excretion), insulin (drives K into cells), adrenal hormones

**Movement in/out of cells:**
- Into cells: insulin, adrenaline (epinephrine), alkalosis
- Out of cells: exercise, cell breakdown, dehydration, acidosis

**Deficiency:** kidneys cannot conserve potassium well — deficiency can develop without regular intake
- Symptoms: muscle weakness, fatigue, mental confusion, heart rhythm disturbances, cramps, palpitations, lightheadedness, rhabdomyolysis risk

**Low potassium causes:** adrenal hyperfunction (↑ aldosterone), diuretics, diarrhea, benign hypertension
- Adrenal stress pattern: ↓ K, normal/↑ Na, ↑ Na:K ratio, ↑ aldosterone, ↑ cortisol, ↓ urinary Cl

**High potassium causes:** adrenal insufficiency, dehydration, tissue destruction (K leaks from damaged cells), metabolic acidosis
- Dehydration pattern: ↑ K, ↑ Na, ↑ HGB, ↑ HCT, ↑ RBC
- Addison's pattern: ↑ K, ↓ Na, ↓ aldosterone, ↓ cortisol

**Practical:**
- 1L coconut water/day ≈ 1–2g potassium (good first-line intervention)
- Add Celtic sea salt (¼–½ tsp/litre water)
- Weight-cut athletes: tightly monitor Na/K together
- If dietary insufficient: potassium citrate (most electrolyte supplements are too low)

**Falsely low:** large glucose intake during tolerance testing (↓ up to 0.4 mmol/L)
**Falsely high:** hemolyzed blood (↑ up to 50%); repeated fist-clenching with tourniquet (↑ 10–20%)

---

### Sodium:Potassium Ratio
- **Conventional:** 30–35 | **Optimal:** 30–35
- Calculated from serum Na and K; reflects adrenal gland function
- Both electrolytes regulated by aldosterone and cortisol from adrenal glands

**High ratio (acute stress):** ↑ aldosterone → Na retention + K excretion → ↑ Na:K → inflammation, pain association
**Low ratio (chronic stress/adrenal fatigue):** ↓ aldosterone → Na loss + K retention → ↓ Na:K → tissue catabolism indicator (cortisol dominating over aldosterone)
- Diet high in sodium can elevate the ratio independently of adrenal status
- High K + low Na in diet associated with significantly lower CVD risk

---

### Chloride (Cl⁻)
- **Conventional:** 98–110 mmol/L | **Optimal:** 100–106 mmol/L
- Principal extracellular anion; closely linked to sodium (levels usually change together)
- **Functions:** acid-base balance, HCl production in stomach, membrane transport, blood pressure, O₂/CO₂ transport by RBCs
- Regulated by aldosterone (promotes reabsorption alongside sodium)
- **Inverse relationship with CO₂:** ↑ Cl → ↓ CO₂ (metabolic acidosis); ↓ Cl → ↑ CO₂ (metabolic alkalosis)

**Low chloride causes:** vomiting/diarrhea, hypochlorhydria, adrenal hypofunction, renal dysfunction, Addison's
- Pattern with hypochlorhydria: ↓ Cl + ↑ BUN + ↑ MCV + ↑ MCH + ↓ serum phosphorus + ↑ CO₂
- Purging indicator: low Cl alone is NOT reliable — use with ↑ CO₂, ↓ K, low urine Cl; urine Na:Cl ratio >1.16 = more specific marker
- Metabolic alkalosis: ↓ Cl + ↑ CO₂ (>30) + ↓ Ca + ↓ K
- Adrenal insufficiency: ↓ Cl + ↓ Na + ↑ K

**High chloride causes:** dehydration, excess salt intake, metabolic acidosis, adrenal stress
- Adrenal stress: ↑ Cl + ↑ Na + ↓ K
- Metabolic acidosis: ↑ Cl + ↓ CO₂ + ↑ anion gap

**Drugs that decrease Cl:** steroids, laxatives, diuretics, theophylline, bicarbonate

---

### Bicarbonate / CO2
- **Conventional:** 19–30 mmol/L | **Optimal:** 25–30 mmol/L
- Serum CO₂ ≈ bicarbonate (same marker on most panels)
- ~75% of CO₂ from cellular respiration is carried as bicarbonate in the blood
- **Function:** alkaline buffer to neutralise metabolic acids
- **Inverse relationship with Cl:** ↑ CO₂ → metabolic alkalosis; ↓ CO₂ → metabolic acidosis

**Low CO₂ causes:**
- Metabolic acidosis: ↓ CO₂ (<25) + ↑ Cl (>106) + ↑ anion gap
- Respiratory alkalosis: hyperventilation, anxiety, stress, high altitude, low blood pressure
- **B1 (thiamine) deficiency:** B1 required for pyruvate → acetyl CoA conversion; deficiency → excess lactate → acid buildup → consumes bicarbonate → ↓ CO₂
- Symptoms: fatigue, poor exercise tolerance, "burnt out" feeling at low training loads

**High CO₂ causes:**
- Metabolic alkalosis: ↑ CO₂ (>30) + ↓ Cl (<100) + ↓ Ca + ↓ K
- **Hypochlorhydria:** ↓ HCl → ↑ H⁺ ions → bicarbonate rises as compensation; **pattern: ↑ CO₂ + ↓ Cl = hypochlorhydria clue**
- Respiratory acidosis: asthma, emphysema, airway obstruction, chest trauma

**B1 Deficiency prevalence context:** 10–20% adults, 76% diabetics, 29% obese, 49% post-bariatric surgery, 40% community-dwelling elderly, 55% cancer patients, 33% CHF patients

---

### Anion Gap
- **Conventional:** 10–20 mmol/L | **Optimal:** 11–16 mmol/L
- **Formula:** (Na + K) − (Cl + CO₂)
- Reflects unmeasured anions (phosphates, sulfates, ketones, proteins, lactate)
- Helpful for assessing acid-base dysfunction

**High anion gap:** ↑ Na/K + ↓ CO₂/Cl → metabolic acidosis, lactic acidosis, ketoacidosis, B1 deficiency
- B1 deficiency mechanism: blocks pyruvate → Krebs cycle → excess lactate → unmeasured anion → ↑ gap
- Associated with: impaired immune function, altered O₂-Hb binding, arrhythmias, hypertension
- Functional sign: deep or rapid breathing at rest = body trying to expel CO₂ to compensate

**Low anion gap (rare):** ↓ Na/K + ↑ CO₂/Cl → hypoalbuminemia, lithium toxicity, multiple myeloma, neoplasms

**Falsely low:** hemolyzed blood, fist-clenching with tourniquet, licorice, high triglycerides or low protein
**Falsely high:** aspirin, diuretics, penicillin; rule out methanol, ethylene glycol, heavy metals

## KIDNEY FUNCTION MARKERS

### Urea / BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen)
- **Conventional:** 2.5–8.92 mmol/L | **Optimal:** 3.57–5.71 mmol/L (10–16 mg/dL)
- End-product of protein metabolism; nitrogen from amino acid breakdown → ammonia → liver converts to urea → kidneys excrete
- **High BUN:** renal impairment, dehydration, high protein intake, increased protein catabolism, adrenal stress (cortisol promotes catabolism), dysbiosis, hypochlorhydria
- **Low BUN:** low protein diet, poor liver function, zinc or B6 deficiency, malnutrition, malabsorption, pancreatic insufficiency

**Athlete context:** high urea + high creatinine in trained clients with high protein intake does NOT mean kidney disease — consider training load and diet first

**BUN/Creatinine ratio:**
- <10 → low protein intake, malabsorption, liver dysfunction, posterior pituitary dysfunction
- 10–20 → possible renal disease (with ↑ creatinine, ↑ uric acid, ↑ phosphorus)

**Pattern — dehydration:** ↑ BUN + ↑ HGB + ↑ HCT + ↑ RBC ± ↑ Na/K (acute); + ↑ albumin + ↑ serum protein (chronic)

**Drugs that decrease BUN:** anabolic steroids, some antibiotics
**Drugs that increase BUN:** diuretics, corticosteroids (prednisone, prednisolone)

---

### Uric Acid / Urate
- **Male conventional:** 0.20–0.47 mmol/L | **Optimal:** 0.21–0.32 mmol/L
- **Female conventional:** 0.15–0.42 mmol/L | **Optimal:** 0.18–0.28 mmol/L
- Waste product from purine breakdown; excreted ~⅔ via kidneys, ~⅓ via stool
- Functions as an **antioxidant** at normal levels — both high and low are linked to disease
- **Labile marker** — affected by daily/seasonal variation, obesity, stress, diet; serial measurements preferred

**Low uric acid:** reduced antioxidant protection → oxidative stress → linked to Parkinson's, MS; also: molybdenum deficiency, B12/folate deficiency, copper deficiency
**High uric acid:** gout (crystal precipitation), atherosclerosis, oxidative stress, rheumatoid arthritis, renal disease, intestinal hyperpermeability, CVD (>75% of heart disease patients with high TGs)

**High UA + ↑ TGs + ↓ HDL + ↑ LDL** → cardiovascular risk pattern
**High UA + ↑ ESR + ↓/normal albumin** → rheumatoid arthritis pattern
**High UA + ↑ BUN + ↑ creatinine + ↑ phosphorus** → renal disease pattern

**Factors affecting uric acid:** genetics, kidney disease, diabetes, diuretics (especially thiazides), aspirin, caffeine/theobromine, heavy metals, purines in diet (dark meats, organ meats, pork, shellfish, legumes)

**Note:** strenuous exercise can temporarily raise uric acid

---

### Creatinine
- **Conventional:** 35–133 µmol/L | **Optimal:** 71–97 µmol/L (0.80–1.10 mg/dL)
- Byproduct of muscle creatine phosphate breakdown — NOT the same as creatine supplement
- ~1–2% of creatine pool converts to creatinine daily; more muscle mass = higher creatinine
- Cleared almost entirely by kidneys; more specific/sensitive marker for renal disease than BUN

**Key athlete context:** elevated creatinine in muscular clients often reflects muscle mass + high protein intake, NOT kidney damage. Doctors may incorrectly advise stopping creatine supplementation.

**Low creatinine:** muscle atrophy, neuromuscular degeneration, liver disease, MS, reduced muscle mass (older adults, malnutrition)
**High creatinine causes:** increased muscle mass, prostate dysfunction (BPH), renal disease, renal insufficiency, creatine supplementation, UTI, high protein diet

**Prostate note:** creatinine >1.1 mg/dL can be an early indicator of BPH if other causes ruled out — look for ↑ monocytes + ↑ LDH isoenzyme #4

**Renal disease pattern:** ↑ creatinine + ↑ BUN (ratio 10–20) + urine specific gravity 1.010–1.016 + ↑ uric acid + ↑ phosphorus + ↑ LDH + ↑ AST

**Falsely low:** high bilirubin, cephalosporins, glucose/histidine/quinidine
**Falsely high:** high vitamin C, cephalosporins, high meat diet

---

### eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)
- **Conventional:** 60–160 mL/min | **Optimal:** 90–120 mL/min
- Estimates how effectively kidneys filter waste from blood
- Calculated from serum creatinine, age, and sex
- Naturally declines with age; females naturally have lower values

**CKD staging:**
- >90: normal
- <90: mild reduction — investigate
- <60: moderate loss — refer to renal specialist
- <15: kidney failure — dialysis/transplant required

**Limitations in athletes:** eGFR is influenced by muscle mass — high muscle mass elevates creatinine and may distort eGFR. Creatinine and eGFR are **late-stage markers** and cannot catch early kidney dysfunction.

**Steroid users:** monitor eGFR + Cystatin C; many premature deaths in bodybuilding may have been preventable with earlier testing

---

### Cystatin-C
- **Conventional:** 0.50–1.00 mg/L | **Optimal:** 0.62–0.90 mg/L
- **Gold standard kidney function marker** — NOT influenced by muscle mass, gender, age, race, diet, infection, or circadian rhythm
- More reliable than creatinine/eGFR in athletes, bodybuilders, steroid users, obese, malnourished, children, elderly
- **High Cystatin-C = poor kidney function** | **Low = good kidney function**
- Early marker — detects kidney dysfunction while creatinine/eGFR are still normal
- Must be specifically requested (not always on standard panels)

**Elevated Cystatin-C also linked to:** cardiovascular disease, heart failure, venous thromboembolism, Alzheimer's, hyperthyroidism, elevated homocysteine, elevated CRP, increased body mass, malignancy, corticosteroid use

**Rule:** any client on anabolic steroids, SARMs, or aggressive protocols → Cystatin-C mandatory

## LIVER & PROTEIN MARKERS

### Total Protein
- **Conventional:** 61–81 g/L | **Optimal:** 69–81 g/L
- Sum of albumin + total globulin; limited clinical value on its own — always examine albumin and globulin individually
- **Low:** hypochlorhydria, digestive dysfunction, liver dysfunction, low protein diet/malnutrition
- **High:** dehydration (hemoconcentration — most common cause)

**Hypochlorhydria pattern:** normal/↓ total protein + ↑ globulin + ↑ BUN + ↓ serum phosphorus + ↑ MCV + ↑ MCH + ↓ Ca + ↓ Fe + ↓ Cl + ↓ ALP

**Liver dysfunction clues:** pain between shoulder blades, greasy food intolerance, easy intoxication with alcohol, headache over the eye, chemical sensitivity, hemorrhoids/varicose veins

**Falsely low:** pregnancy (especially 3rd trimester), prolonged bed rest
**Falsely high:** hemolysis, dehydration

---

### Globulin
- **Conventional:** 19–37 g/L | **Optimal:** 24–28 g/L
- Family of proteins including antibodies (IgG, IgA, IgM, IgD, IgE) and transport proteins; synthesised in liver or immune cells
- Proxy for immune function; fractions: alpha-1 (~10%), alpha-2 (~20%), beta (~30% — includes transferrin, SHBG), gamma (~40–45% — immunoglobulins)
- Total value cannot identify which fraction is abnormal; protein electrophoresis needed if >3.5 or <2.0 g/dL

**Low globulin:** immunodeficiency, digestive inflammation (chronic), kidney/liver disease, reduced antibody production (low sIgA)

**High globulin causes:** hypochlorhydria, liver damage/infections (hepatitis, cirrhosis), oxidative stress, immune activation, autoimmune disease, IBD, heavy metal/chemical toxicity, high estrogen/oral contraceptives
- Oxidative stress pattern: ↑ globulin + sudden ↓ cholesterol + ↓ lymphocytes + ↓ albumin + ↓ platelets + ↑ uric acid + ↑ bilirubin

**Albumin:Globulin Ratio**
- **Conventional:** 1.0–2.5 | **Optimal:** 1.4–2.1
- ↓ ratio (more common): ↓ albumin + ↑ globulin → liver dysfunction or immune activation
- ↑ ratio (uncommon): usually dehydration

---

### Albumin
- **Conventional:** 36–51 g/L | **Optimal:** 45–50 g/L
- Major blood protein produced in liver; negative acute-phase reactant (↓ when inflammation is high)
- **Functions:** maintains osmotic pressure, transports hormones and drugs, water distribution
- Proxy for nutritional status, liver health, and inflammation

**Low albumin causes:** hypochlorhydria, liver dysfunction, oxidative stress, systemic inflammation, vitamin C need, pregnancy, kidney disease (proteinuria)
- Oxidative stress pattern: ↓ albumin + ↓ lymphocytes + sudden ↓ cholesterol + ↑ globulin + ↑ uric acid + ↓ platelets + ↑ bilirubin

**High albumin:** dehydration (most common — always consider first)
- Dehydration pattern: ↑ albumin + ↑ HGB + ↑ HCT + ↑ RBC ± ↑ Na/K (acute); + ↑ BUN + ↑ serum protein (chronic)

---

### Bilirubin
- **Total conventional:** 3.42–20.52 µmol/L | **Optimal:** 8.55–15.39 µmol/L
- **Direct (conjugated) optimal:** 1.71–2.56 µmol/L
- **Indirect (unconjugated) optimal:** 6.84–12.82 µmol/L
- Byproduct of RBC breakdown → transported to liver → conjugated (water-soluble) → excreted via bile

**Differentiating fractions:**
- ↑ indirect/unconjugated: excess RBC destruction (hemolysis), Gilbert's syndrome (genetic phase II detox defect — ≥90% as indirect fraction + normal GGT/AST/ALT)
- ↑ direct/conjugated: liver dysfunction, biliary obstruction
- ↑ both: biliary tract obstruction/calculi (+ ↑ GGT + ↑ ALP + ↑ LDH)

**Low bilirubin:** oxidative stress (bilirubin is an antioxidant), systemic inflammation, higher atherosclerotic plaque risk, fatty liver risk, NASH/liver fibrosis association

**Gallbladder dysfunction:** ↑ bilirubin + ↑ ALP ± ↑ GGT; cholesterol may be ↑ or ↓
**Liver dysfunction:** ↑ bilirubin + ↑ albumin + ↑ ALT

**Falsely low:** sunlight exposure of sample, high-fat meal prior, air bubbles in sample
**Falsely high:** prolonged fasting

---

### ALP (Alkaline Phosphatase)
- **Conventional:** 35–150 IU/L | **Optimal:** 45–100 IU/L
- Zinc and magnesium-dependent metalloenzyme; produced in bone, liver, intestines, skin, placenta
- Important for bone mineralisation and teeth development
- **Low ALP → think zinc and/or magnesium deficiency** (also Wilson's disease, pernicious anemia, hypothyroidism, very low fat/protein diet)

**High ALP causes:** biliary obstruction (+ ↑ GGT), liver cell damage (+ ↑ bilirubin + ↑ AST + ↑ GGT), bone disease/fracture/Paget's, vitamin C need, shingles (herpes zoster), metastatic carcinoma of bone
- Biliary obstruction: ALP + GGT markedly elevated vs ALT
- Bone growth/fracture: physiological elevation in children/adolescents and during fracture healing
- Bone metastases prevalence: prostate 70–90%, breast 50%, lung/kidney/thyroid ~30%

**Low ALP pattern:** ↓ ALP + ↓ WBC or RBC zinc → zinc deficiency; confirm with zinc taste test

**May decrease levels:** estrogen, estrogen + androgens
**May increase levels:** allopurinol, colchicine, antibiotics, fluorides

---

### GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase)
- **Conventional:** 3–85 IU/L | **Optimal:** 10–17 IU/L
- Highest concentration in liver cells; also present in kidney, prostate, pancreas, biliary epithelium
- **Role in glutathione (GSH) metabolism:** GGT breaks down and recycles GSH; elevated GGT may indicate GSH depletion → reduced antioxidant defence
- GGT is a **predictive biomarker for cardiovascular disease, diabetes, metabolic syndrome, and all-cause mortality**
- Elevated in alcoholism; can be ↑ up to 50% in obese patients
- Men may have high-normal GGT due to prostate content

**Low GGT:** vitamin B6 (P-5-P) deficiency and/or magnesium deficiency
- B6 deficiency pattern: ↓ GGT + ↓ AST + ↓ ALT + ↓ MCV/MCH with normal Fe and ferritin (B6 deficiency anemia)

**High GGT causes:**
- Biliary obstruction: ↑ GGT >5× + ↑ ALP → biliary tree involvement
- Biliary stasis: ↑ GGT + ↑ bilirubin + ↑ ALP + ↑ cholesterol; AST/ALT normal or ↑
- Liver cell damage (hepatitis, cirrhosis, alcoholic hepatitis): GGT often 3–5× normal (200–300)
- Excessive alcohol: ↑ GGT + ↑ TGs → rule out alcohol as contributor
- Pancreatitis/pancreatic insufficiency: ↑ GGT ≥5× → suspect pancreatic pathology

**TUDCA 750mg/day** has been shown to massively decrease ALT, AST, and GGT
**GSH + brain health:** NAC (glutathione precursor) shown to reduce depressive episodes in bipolar disorder

---

### ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase)
- **Conventional:** 6–29 IU/L | **Optimal:** 10–26 IU/L
- Mostly found in liver → most liver-specific enzyme (more specific than AST)
- Released when hepatocytes are stressed or damaged
- Remains elevated longer than AST after acute injury — useful for chronic vs acute differentiation

**Low ALT:** vitamin B6 deficiency (pattern: ↓ ALT + ↓ GGT + ↓ AST), low protein intake, early-stage fatty liver (steatosis) — pattern: ↓ ALT + ↑ cholesterol + ↑ LDL + ↑ TGs + ↓ HDL

**High ALT → problem likely inside the liver if ALT > AST and GGT**
- Fatty liver (steatosis): ALT > AST and GGT; ↑ LDH and ALP likely
- Liver dysfunction, biliary obstruction, liver infection (hepatitis, CMV, EBV)
- Severe hepatocellular destruction: ALT can rise 30–50× above normal
- Excessive muscle breakdown/weight training can also raise ALT

---

### AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase)
- **Conventional:** 10–35 IU/L | **Optimal:** 10–26 IU/L
- Present in skeletal muscle, liver, heart, kidney, lungs — less liver-specific than ALT
- More specific for **cardiovascular-related injury** than biliary/primary liver problems
- Rises within ~12 hours of severe cellular damage; normalises in 3–6 days

**Low AST:** vitamin B6 deficiency (pattern: ↓ AST + ↓ GGT + ↓ ALT), low protein intake, anemia, low mitochondrial function

**High AST → problem likely outside the liver or biliary tree if AST > ALT and GGT**
- Cardiovascular: developing CHF pattern → ↑ AST > ALT + normal/↑ GGT + ↑ ALP + ↓ CO₂ + ↑ ESR + ↑ uric acid
- Acute MI: AST rises 4–10× above reference, peaks ~24h post-MI, normalises in 3–4 days
- Liver cell damage/cirrhosis: AST 10–100× above reference with ↑ ALT + ↑ GGT + ↓ albumin
- Muscle breakdown: high AST from weight training, trauma, polymyositis
- EBV/CMV/infectious mono: AST markedly ↑ 5–14 days after onset; ↑ ALP + ↑ LDH in ~95% of cases

**Falsely low:** slight decrease in pregnancy (altered B6 metabolism)
**Falsely high:** salicylate use, alcohol consumption

## MINERALS — BIOCHEMISTRY PANEL

### Calcium
- **Conventional:** 2.15–2.60 mmol/L | **Optimal:** 2.22–2.38 mmol/L
- ~99% stored in bones/teeth; 1% tightly regulated in blood
- Serum calcium forms: ~50% ionized (biologically active), ~40% protein-bound (mainly albumin), ~10% bound to other substances
- Regulated by PTH, vitamin D, magnesium

**Albumin correction:** For every 1 g/dL ↓ in albumin, add 0.8 mg/dL to measured calcium. Low albumin is the most common cause of low serum calcium. Check for "Corrected Calcium" on the panel.

**Low calcium causes:** low vitamin D, low magnesium, hypochlorhydria, poor dietary intake, high stress, low albumin, kidney damage
- Parathyroid hypofunction: ↓ Ca + ↑ phosphorus → confirm with serum PTH
- Calcium deficiency pattern: ↓ Ca + ↑ phosphorus + ↓ vitamin D

**High calcium causes:** high vitamin D supplementation, high calcium supplementation, chronic stress, dehydration, overactive parathyroid (↑ Ca + ↓ phosphorus → check PTH)
- Ca >2.65 mmol/L → check serum PTH + consider endocrinology referral
- Hypothyroidism: ↑ Ca + ↑ TSH (primary) or ↑ Ca + ↓ TSH (secondary/pituitary)

**Subjective signs of calcium need:** joint stiffness, muscle cramps, poor sleep
**Key cofactors for calcium metabolism:** vitamin D, magnesium, phosphorus, vitamins A/B/C, unsaturated fatty acids, iodine

---

### Phosphorus / Phosphate
- **Conventional:** 0.81–1.45 mmol/L | **Optimal:** 0.84–1.13 mmol/L
- ~85% stored in bone with calcium; remainder intracellular
- Forms the "P" in ATP — critical for energy production
- **Inverse relationship with calcium:** ↑ Ca → ↓ phosphorus; ↓ Ca → ↑ phosphorus
- Regulated by PTH (increases bone resorption and renal excretion of phosphate)
- Serum phosphorus is a general marker for digestion; decreases with hypochlorhydria and high refined carbohydrate intake

**Low phosphorus causes:** parathyroid hyperfunction (↓ P + significantly ↑ Ca → hyperparathyroidism), hypochlorhydria, hyperinsulinism (glucose + P enter cells together), high refined carbohydrate diet, vitamin D deficiency
- High refined carbs: repeated post-meal phosphate shifts into cells deplete long-term stores + co-factors for carbohydrate metabolism

**High phosphorus causes:** parathyroid hypofunction (↑ P + ↓ Ca), bone growth/repair (normal in children), excessive soda/phosphoric acid intake, renal insufficiency (↑ P + ↑ BUN + normal/↑ creatinine — often an early overlooked sign)

**Subjective signs of phosphorus need:** joint stiffness upon waking, cold hands and feet, joint soreness after inactivity that improves with movement

**Natural variations:** higher in afternoon/evening (up to 2× morning values); highest in May–June, lowest in winter
**Falsely low:** after high-carbohydrate meal
**Falsely high:** laxatives/enemas with sodium phosphate; hemolysis

---

### Magnesium
- **Serum conventional:** 0.62–1.04 mmol/L | **Optimal:** ≥0.90 mmol/L
- **RBC conventional:** 1.66–2.82 mmol/L | **Optimal:** 2.48–2.82 mmol/L
- Essential mineral for 300+ enzymatic reactions
- Only ~1% of total body magnesium is extracellular (what serum blood tests measure) — can appear "normal" while deficient
- **RBC magnesium is more accurate** for assessing true body status
- <50% of US population consumes adequate magnesium; subclinical deficiency common even at "normal" serum levels

**Magnesium supports 6 major systems:** heart (rhythm, energy), brain/nerves (calms NS, reduces overexcitation), muscles (prevents cramps, regulates contraction), clotting/blood, bones (with calcium/vitamin D), inflammation/immunity

**High stress = high magnesium demand** — anxiety, burnout, sleep issues accelerate depletion

**Low magnesium causes:** low dietary intake, malabsorption, diarrhea, alcoholism, adrenal hyperfunction, liver dysfunction, diuretic use, diabetes
- Symptoms: muscle cramps, anxiety, hypertension, irritability, vertigo, irregular heartbeat, depression, insomnia, poor blood sugar regulation
- Low Mg drives hypertension via: ↑ catecholamine release + Na retention + ↑ cholesterol → ↑ CVD risk; linked to AFib, atherosclerosis, thrombosis

**High RBC magnesium:** usually renal dysfunction (impaired excretion), excessive supplementation, thyroid hypofunction (↑ RBC Mg + ↑ TSH or ↓ TSH depending on type)

**Supplementation evidence:**
- ≥240 mg/day: ↓ BP in uncontrolled medicated hypertensives
- ≥600 mg/day: ↓ BP in uncontrolled untreated hypertensives
- Also improves lipids, CRP, fasting glucose, insulin resistance
- UL (supplement): 350 mg/day (set conservatively for mild diarrhea risk at 360–380 mg/day)

**Falsely high:** aspirin, lithium therapy, hemolysis, dehydration
**Falsely low:** calcium gluconate use, hemodilution, cisplatin, amphotericin

### Oestradiol (E2)
- **Follicular:** 69–529 pmol/L | **Luteal:** 206–786 pmol/L | **Ovulation:** 235–1311 pmol/L | **Post-menopausal:** 0–114 pmol/L
- Most potent and clinically measured estrogen form (alongside E1, E3)
- ~98% bound to SHBG; biologically active as free form
- **Key note:** don't chase numbers — understand symptoms

**Functions:** female sexual maturation, menstrual cycle regulation, protein synthesis/tissue repair, cardiovascular function, bone formation/density, cognitive health, cellular proliferation

**Low E2 causes:** high stress, low body fat, menopause/perimenopause, thyroid dysfunction, low fat intake, nutrient deficiencies, overtraining, malnutrition
- Bone density risk: low E2 = significant osteoporosis risk factor
- Hormone-linked migraines: declining E2 triggers migraines; sustained ↑ E2 (pregnancy) often reduces headache frequency

**High E2 causes:** low progesterone (estrogen dominance), hormonal birth control, poor liver detox, constipation/dysbiosis, environmental toxins, insulin resistance, obesity, inflammation
- ↑ E2 linked to higher breast and endometrial cancer risk (acts as "fuel" for estrogen-responsive tissues)

### Progesterone
- **Female follicular:** 0–3.18 nmol/L | **Luteal:** 8.27–68.37 nmol/L | **Post-menopausal:** 0–1.59 nmol/L
- **Male conventional:** 0.64–4.13 nmol/L | **Optimal:** 1.21–2.86 nmol/L
- Produced in ovaries, adrenal glands, placenta
- **Functions:** supports bone density, protects against proliferation of breast/uterine cells, coats nerve fibres (calming effect), supports pregnancy

**Note on the pill:** natural progesterone is suppressed by OCP — synthetic progestins have different characteristics and risk profile. Testing while on pill gives inaccurate results.

**Low progesterone causes:** chronic stress, high estrogen, PCOS, lack of exercise, menopause, oral contraceptives, anovulation

- Pattern: progesterone <0.64 nmol/L + estradiol >1292 pmol/L → suspect estrogen dominance
- If suspected: run 30-day salivary estrogen/progesterone panel or DUTCH test

**Symptoms of low progesterone:** anxiety, depression, sleep problems, fatigue, memory loss, fibrocystic breasts, headaches, hot flashes, night sweats, low libido, mood swings, irregular/heavy periods, lowered immunity, high BP, bone loss

### Total Testosterone
- **Male conventional:** 8.67–38.14 nmol/L | **Optimal:** 24.27–38.14 nmol/L
- **Female conventional:** 0.07–1.56 nmol/L | **Optimal:** 1.21–1.56 nmol/L
- Primary androgen; after 25–30 y/o, slowly declines (~60% loss between ages 40–70)
- Signalling hormone — binds to receptors; effects cap out once receptors are fully utilised
- **Don't chase the number** — aim for quality of life and good energy

**Testosterone fractions:**
- Bound to SHBG (~55%): mostly inactive
- Bound to albumin (~44%): loosely bound, some bioavailability
- Free (~1–3%): biologically active, can bind receptors
- **Bioavailable T** = free T + albumin-bound T

**Low testosterone causes (male):** primary hypogonadism, Klinefelter, mumps, hemochromatosis, testicular injury, pituitary disorders, obesity, aging, metabolic syndrome, diabetes
- Associated with: Alzheimer's risk, increased stroke/CVD risk, ↓ libido, ED, loss of muscle, ↑ abdominal fat, ↓ bone density, depression

**High testosterone causes:** anabolic steroids, testosterone-producing tumors, PCOS (female), congenital adrenal hyperplasia
- Chronically ↑ T → ↑ prostate carcinoma risk
- In females: hirsutism, PCOS, insulin resistance, ↓ estrogen

**Drugs that decrease testosterone:** alcohol, oral contraceptives, magnesium (high dose), cimetidine, gemfibrozil, ketoconazole, metformin, pravastatin, prednisone, spironolactone, tetracycline, verapamil

### Free Testosterone
- **Male conventional:** 159–777 pmol/L | **Optimal:** 521–777 pmol/L
- **Female conventional:** 0.69–17.34 pmol/L | **Optimal:** 11.27–15.95 pmol/L
- **Bioavailable T (male) optimal:** 3.00–19.94 nmol/L
- The most important testosterone number — what's actually available to bind androgen receptors
- "100 coins on a table, 50 are glued down — you only have 50 to use"

**What causes low free T with normal total T?**
SHBG is usually high — caused by inflammation, sleep deprivation, nutritional deficiencies, medications, overtraining, extended intermittent fasting

**Low bioavailable T associations:** metabolic syndrome, diabetes, Alzheimer's, CVD, ↑ hypertension risk, prostate cancer (BT <104 ng/dL = 4.9× higher prostate cancer detection risk), rheumatoid arthritis, reduced cognition/visuospatial function

**High free T in females:** PCOS, adrenal hyperplasia, insulin resistance, ↓ SHBG, hirsutism

### Free Androgen Index (FAI)
- **Formula:** (Total Testosterone ÷ SHBG) × 100
- Estimates free (active) testosterone; particularly useful in females
- FAI correlates better than free T measurement for detecting hypoandrogenism in women
- FAI superior to total T alone for diagnosing hyperandrogenism in PCOS

**High FAI (female):** PCOS, ovarian tumor, adrenal hyperplasia → masculinisation, hirsutism, irregular periods, insulin resistance
**Low FAI (female):** extreme fatigue, bone loss, low libido

### SHBG (Sex Hormone-Binding Globulin)
- **Male conventional:** 10–50 nmol/L | **Optimal:** 40–46 nmol/L
- **Female conventional:** 17–124 nmol/L | **Optimal:** 50–80 nmol/L
- Glycoprotein produced mainly in liver; also in testes, uterus, brain, placenta
- "The glue holding the coins to the table" — determines how much T is bioavailable
- SHBG levels **increase with age** (opposite trend to sex hormones); emerging evidence suggests it acts as a signalling molecule, not just a carrier

**Low SHBG:** associated with metabolic syndrome, CVD, ↑ TGs, ↑ LDL, ↑ fasting insulin, ↑ blood glucose, ↑ uric acid, ↓ HDL, ↑ CRP

**High SHBG causes:** calorie deficit, anorexia/very low BMI, type 1 diabetes (no insulin to block SHBG), hyperthyroidism, alcohol consumption, aging
- ↑ SHBG + low free T → undiagnosed androgen deficiency even with normal total T
- ↑ SHBG in women → ↓ available T → low energy, muscle loss, ↓ bone density, ↓ libido
- **The pill (combined OCP with ethinylestradiol):** mean decrease of 61% in free T levels in healthy young women (systematic review, 42 studies); SHBG skyrockets; many women experience permanent low libido, poor recovery, difficulty building muscle without knowing the cause

**SHBG elevated by:** thyroid hormone replacement, exogenous estrogen therapy, undernourishment

### LH (Luteinising Hormone)
- **Male conventional:** 1.5–9.3 IU/L | **Optimal:** 1.5–6.15 IU/L
- **Female — follicular:** 1.9–12.5 | **Luteal:** 0.5–16.9 | **Ovulation:** 8.7–76.3 | **Post-menopausal:** 10–54.7 IU/L
- Stimulates Leydig cells (testes) to produce testosterone; stimulates ovulation in females
- Feeds back to hypothalamus/pituitary to downregulate own production (negative feedback)

**Low LH causes:** exogenous testosterone therapy (expected — HPG axis suppression), anterior pituitary hypofunction, secondary hypogonadism (obesity, chronic stress, medications), anorexia, malnutrition, anabolic steroids
- Pituitary insufficiency: ↓ LH + ↓ FSH + ↓ TSH → full pituitary panel needed

**High LH causes:** primary hypogonadism (testes failing — ↑ LH but ↓ T), Klinefelter syndrome, aging/andropause, PCOS (females — LH:FSH ratio often >2), premature ovarian insufficiency
- In testosterone therapy: persistently ↑ LH may signal inadequate suppression or underlying primary testicular defect

**Drugs that decrease LH:** testosterone therapy, HRT, oral contraceptives, anticonvulsants, digoxin, naloxone

### FSH (Follicle-Stimulating Hormone)
- **Male conventional:** 1.4–12.8 IU/L | **Optimal:** 1.6–8.0 IU/L
- **Female — follicular:** 2.5–10.2 | **Luteal:** 1.5–9.1 | **Ovulation:** 3.1–17.7 | **Post-menopausal:** 23–116.3 IU/L
- Stimulates Sertoli cells in testes to produce sperm (via ABP); stimulates follicle development in females
- ↑ FSH during menopause is expected; levels ~30–40 IU/mL confirm menopause

**Low FSH causes:** anterior pituitary hypofunction (+ ↓ LH + ↓ TSH), testosterone therapy (expected HPG suppression), anorexia, malnutrition, anabolic steroids

**High FSH causes:** menopause/perimenopause, primary testicular failure (Klinefelter, post-orchiectomy, idiopathic), ovarian hypofunction/failure, PCOS (some cases — though LH usually higher), andropause, inadequate testosterone therapy suppression

**Drugs that decrease FSH:** testosterone therapy, HRT, oral contraceptives, anticonvulsants, digoxin, naloxone

### Prolactin
- **Female conventional:** 63.83–638.30 mIU/L | **Optimal:** 63.83–212.77 mIU/L
- **Male conventional:** 42.55–382.98 mIU/L | **Optimal:** 42.55–212.77 mIU/L
- Produced by pituitary gland; primarily stimulates breast development and lactation
- Regulated by dopamine (inhibits) and TRH (stimulates); follows diurnal rhythm (peak at night)
- **High prolactin → anovulation pathway:** ↑ prolactin → ↓ dopamine → ↓ GnRH → ↓ FSH + LH → ↓ ovulation

**Low prolactin:** hypopituitarism, male sexual dysfunction (ED, premature ejaculation, ↓ sperm count/motility)

**High prolactin causes:** pregnancy/breastfeeding, abnormal nipple discharge, prolactinoma (most common benign pituitary tumor — ≥50 ng/mL warrants evaluation), PCOS, glucose regulation impairment (↑ insulin resistance)

### Cortisol
- **AM conventional:** 110.35–606.94 nmol/L | **Optimal:** 275.88–413.82 nmol/L
- **PM conventional:** 82.76–469.00 nmol/L | **Optimal:** 110.35–275.88 nmol/L
- Primary glucocorticoid; produced from cholesterol in adrenal cortex; ~90% bound to CBG/albumin
- **Diurnal rhythm:** highest early morning, declines ~50% by evening — collection time is critical
- Regulated by HPA axis: ↓ cortisol → ↑ CRH (hypothalamus) → ↑ ACTH (pituitary) → ↑ cortisol (adrenals)

**Functions:** blood sugar balance, metabolism regulation, anti-inflammation, blood pressure control, gluconeogenesis, protein catabolism

**Low cortisol:** adrenal fatigue/insufficiency, autoimmune conditions
- Pattern: ↑ K (>4.5), normal/↓ Na (<135), ↓ Cl (<100), ↓ Na:K ratio, ↓ aldosterone, ↑ urinary Cl
- Confirm with salivary cortisol (daily rhythm assessment)

**High cortisol:** adrenal stress, fasting, hypoglycemia, dieting phases
- Pattern: ↑ Na (>142), ↓ K (<4.0), ↓ urinary Cl
- If markedly elevated → rule out adrenal adenoma
- High cortisol promotes protein catabolism → ↑ BUN

**Falsely increased:** oral contraceptive use, estrogen therapy

## THYROID PANEL

### TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone)
- **Conventional:** 0.40–4.50 mIU/L | **Optimal:** 1.00–2.00 mIU/L
- Produced by anterior pituitary — NOT a thyroid hormone; reflects how loud the pituitary is signalling the thyroid
- ↑ TSH = body needs more thyroid hormone | ↓ TSH = body needs less (or pituitary dysfunction)
- Most sensitive marker for **primary** hypothyroidism

**Reference range evolution:**
- Historical upper limit: 10.0 mIU/L (now outdated)
- Current: 0.45–4.5 mIU/L
- NACB: 95% of healthy individuals without thyroid disease have TSH <2.5 mIU/L
- TSH 2.5–4.5: linked to obesity, hypertriglyceridemia, metabolic syndrome, depression, thyroid autoantibodies
- Optimal: 1.0–1.9 mIU/L (lowest thyroid dysfunction risk prospectively)

**High TSH — Primary Hypothyroidism/Hashimoto's:**
Symptoms: fatigue, depression, cold intolerance, weight gain, constipation, irregular periods, dry/itchy skin, hair loss, concentration difficulties, elevated cholesterol, slowed heart rate/movement, delayed ankle reflexes, puffiness in ankles
- Pattern: ↑ TSH + normal/↓ T4 + ↓ free T4/T3 + ↑ cholesterol + ↑ TGs
- Hashimoto's: ↑ anti-TPO antibodies + ↑ anti-TG antibodies (autoimmune destruction)
- Thyroid nutrient support: iodine (150µg), selenium (converts T4→T3; also protects thyroid from oxidative damage), glutathione (NAC, liposomal), tyrosine (250–500mg 1–2×/day)

**Low TSH:**
- Hyperthyroidism: ↓ TSH + ↑ T3 + ↑ free T3 + ↑ T4 (consider Graves' disease, thyroid antibodies)
- Secondary hypothyroidism (pituitary hypofunction): ↓ TSH + ↓/normal T4/T3 + ↑ TGs + ↑ cholesterol + ↑ BUN + ↑ Ca (often mistaken for primary hypothyroid)
- Tertiary hypothyroidism (hypothalamic dysfunction): ↓ TSH + ↓ T4 + ↓ T3; linked to chronic fatigue syndrome, fibromyalgia, mitochondrial dysfunction
- Heavy metal burden: aluminum, mercury, cadmium disrupt thyroid receptor activity; check MCH + MCHC + uric acid; confirm with hair analysis

---

### Free T4 (FT4)
- **Conventional:** 10.30–23.17 pmol/L | **Optimal:** 12.87–19.30 pmol/L
- Inactive form; produced by thyroid, stored in follicles or released into circulation (mostly bound to TBG)
- ~70% converted peripherally to either T3 (active) or rT3 (inactive)
- "Free" = unbound fraction (~0.03–0.05% of total T4); available for conversion
- T4→T3 conversion requires: iodine, selenium, tyrosine. Deficiencies should be ruled out before thyroid treatment.
- High estrogen (OCP, pregnancy) → ↑ TBG → ↑ total T4 (falsely elevated); free T4 more accurate in this context

**Cofactors for T4 synthesis:** zinc, copper, vitamins A, B2, B3, B6, C

**Low FT4 causes:** primary hypothyroidism, iodine deficiency (+ ↓ total T4, normal/↑ T3, normal/↑ TSH), secondary hypothyroidism (pituitary hypofunction — ↓ TSH, ↓ FT4, ↓ FT3), protein deficiency, medications, heavy metals

**High FT4 causes:** hyperthyroidism (↓ TSH + ↑ T3 + ↑ FT3), thyroid hormone replacement therapy

**Drugs that decrease FT4:** androgens, anticonvulsants, salicylates, T3 treatment, anticoagulants
**Drugs that increase FT4:** oral contraceptives, exogenous estrogen, heroin/methadone, propranolol

---

### Free T3 (FT3)
- **Conventional:** 3.53–6.45 pmol/L | **Optimal:** 4.61–5.38 pmol/L
- **Active** thyroid hormone; ~8–10% of circulating T3 is "free" (biologically active)
- ~20% secreted directly by thyroid; ~80% converted from T4 in liver, gut, kidneys, and cells
- Directly influences mitochondria → ↑ ATP via oxidative phosphorylation; enhances cardiac output
- Conversion T4→T3 requires selenium-dependent 5'-deiodinase

**Low FT3 causes:** primary hypothyroidism, selenium deficiency (↓ T4→T3 conversion), Thyroid Conversion Syndrome (normal TSH + ↓ T3 + ↓ FT3 + ↑ rT3 — triggered by stress/malnutrition/low-calorie diet), secondary hypothyroidism, Euthyroid Sick Syndrome (+ liver/kidney dysfunction)

**High FT3 causes:** hyperthyroidism/Graves' disease (↓ TSH + ↑ total T3 + ↑ FT3 + ↑ FT4); iodine deficiency compensatory ↑ in T3

---

### Reverse T3 (rT3)
- **Conventional:** 0.12–0.38 nmol/L | **Optimal:** 0.15–0.38 nmol/L
- Inactive form derived from T4 — functions as a **metabolic brake**
- ~95% generated peripherally; ~45–50% of daily T4 normally converts to rT3
- rT3 can **block T3 receptors** without activating them → limits ATP production; may also inhibit 5'-deiodinase → further reduces T4→T3 conversion

**Elevated rT3 triggers:** high stress/cortisol, chronic illness, inflammation, vitamin deficiencies, fasting, yo-yo dieting, poor nutrition, calorie restriction, lack of exercise, alcohol intake
- Specific triggers: glucocorticoids, diabetes, ↑ cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha), ↑ free radicals, heavy metal exposure (cadmium, mercury, lead), prolonged illness, ↑ epinephrine/norepinephrine

**Low rT3:** not clinically significant

---

### TPO Antibodies (TPOAb)
- **Conventional:** 0–9 IU/mL | **Optimal:** 0–6.8 IU/mL
- TPO = enzyme in thyroid cells that attaches iodine to tyrosine to form T4
- ↑ TPOAb = immune system attacking thyroid → suggests autoimmune thyroid disease (Hashimoto's or Graves')
- **Testing method matters:** Quest and Labcorp use different techniques — may give different results for same patient; use same lab for serial testing

**Early Hashimoto's:** autoantibodies → T4 spills into circulation → short-term hyperthyroid phase → ↓ TSH + ↑ FT4/FT3 + ↑ TPOAb + ↑ TGAb; adrenals stressed
**Late Hashimoto's:** progressive thyroid destruction → ↓ T4 → hypothyroidism

**Iron-thyroid connection:** iron required to make T4; thyroid hormones regulate iron metabolism via transferrin; iron necessary for TPO enzyme function and T4→T3 conversion

---

### Thyroglobulin Antibodies (TgAb)
- **Conventional:** 0–1 IU/mL | **Optimal:** 0–1 IU/mL
- Antibodies targeting thyroglobulin (essential for T3/T4 synthesis)
- ↑ TgAb = immune system attacking thyroid → Hashimoto's or Graves' disease
- Also seen in papillary and follicular thyroid cancers (not medullary or anaplastic)
- False negatives can occur due to fluctuating immune activity; small % of healthy people may have TgAb
- Test alongside TPOAb for full autoimmune thyroid picture

## THYROID — CLINICAL FRAMEWORK (Functional Approach)

### 3 Types of Hypothyroidism
1. **Classical:** ↑ TSH + normal/↓ T4 + ↓ T3; well-functioning HPT axis
2. **Subclinical:** normal or mildly ↑ TSH + normal T4/T3; may have symptoms; often a decade before overt diagnosis
3. **Autoimmune (AITD/Hashimoto's):** positive antibodies (TPOAb, TgAb); fluctuating labs; 4 stages

### Stages of Autoimmune Thyroid Disease (AITD)
1. Genetic susceptibility + immune dysregulation (genes load the gun; environment pulls the trigger; ~30% genetic, ~70% environmental)
2. Environmental trigger activates immune dysregulation (EBV/glandular fever, COVID-19, food/environmental sensitivities, gut dysbiosis, celiac disease, estrogen dominance, stress, nutrient deficiencies)
3. **Stage 1 AITD:** positive antibodies detected; TSH still in range; asymptomatic; gland not yet damaged — **most important time to identify**
4. **Overt hypothyroidism:** progressive gland destruction; ↑ TSH + ↓ T4/T3 + all clinical symptoms

### 9 Root Causes Checklist
1. Nutrient deficiencies (iodine, selenium, vitamin D, iron, B12, zinc)
2. Aggressive caloric restriction / yo-yo dieting (can trigger gene expression even if not current)
3. Chronic stress (HPA axis dysregulation → affects HPT axis)
4. Estrogen dominance (especially 35–50 y/o; progesterone decline → estrogen unopposed)
5. Inflammation (check CRP, ESR)
6. Viral infections — especially EBV/glandular fever (always ask about history)
7. COVID-19 (direct thyroid ACE receptor affinity)
8. Gut dysbiosis / celiac disease (60% T4→T3 conversion occurs in liver/gut)
9. Environmental toxins (BPA, heavy metals, fluoride in water)

### Key Testing Protocol
Full thyroid panel: **TSH + free T4 + free T3 + TPOAb + TgAb + rT3**
Supporting markers: iodine (urine, creatinine-corrected), selenium, vitamin D, ferritin, iron, B12, cortisol, estrogen/progesterone, celiac screen

### System-Wide Effects of Low Thyroid Function
- **Metabolism:** ↓ BMR → fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance
- **Cardiovascular:** ↓ heart rate + cardiac output → cold hands/feet, low BP, sluggish circulation
- **Musculoskeletal:** muscle weakness, cramps, slow recovery, joint stiffness
- **GI:** ↓ gut motility → constipation, bloating, sluggish digestion
- **Reproductive:** irregular periods, anovulation, hypothalamic amenorrhea, infertility, PMS
- **Neurological:** brain fog, poor memory, low mood, depression
- **Liver:** ↓ phase 2 detoxification, ↑ cholesterol (impaired clearance), ↓ T4→T3 conversion
- **Skin/Hair:** dry skin, hair thinning, brittle nails, poor wound healing

### Practical Clinical Notes
- Many patients have normal TSH but tanked T4/T3 → HPT axis offline → TSH not identifying the threat
- TSH alone is insufficient — test T4 and T3 as a minimum
- T4→T3 conversion: 60% in liver, remainder peripherally (brain, skeletal muscle, thyroid)
- Thyroid hormone resistance, low T3 syndromes, and impaired conversion all present with hypothyroid symptoms despite "normal" labs
- Low basal body temperature is a useful tracking tool alongside labs

- **Active B12 (holoTC) conventional:** 25–165 pmol/L | **Optimal:** 54–165 pmol/L
- Essential for DNA synthesis, RBC maturation, myelin sheath formation, nerve maintenance
- Stored in liver (large reserves — takes years for symptoms with B12 deficiency)
- Exclusively from animal-based foods (meat, eggs, dairy)

**Testing context:**
- Total serum B12: includes both active (10–30%) and inactive (bound to haptocorrin) fractions — good for screening, misleading for early deficiency
- **Active B12 (holotranscobalamin/holoTC):** most reliable early deficiency marker — directly delivered to tissues
- **MMA (methylmalonic acid):** highly sensitive functional marker — elevated = B12 insufficient for cellular metabolism (unless kidney disease)
- **Homocysteine:** elevated in B12 (or folate or B6) deficiency

**Deficiency thresholds:**
- <147 pmol/L: severe deficiency
- 148–258 pmol/L: moderate deficiency
- 259–331 pmol/L: early-stage deficiency
- <402 pmol/L: investigate further if trending down

**Low B12 pattern:** ↑ MCV + ↑ MCH + ↑ RDW + ↓ Hb + ↑ homocysteine + low active B12
**Malabsorption pattern:** low B12 + low iron + low zinc + low copper → systemic digestive absorption issue

**Causes of low B12:** pernicious anemia (autoimmune loss of intrinsic factor), vegan/vegetarian diet, low stomach acid (hypochlorhydria), PPI/antacid use, metformin, SIBO, celiac disease, gastric bypass

**Paradoxically high B12 with deficiency symptoms:** functional deficiency — B12 not taken up at tissue level; also seen in liver disease, myeloproliferative neoplasms, diabetes, heart failure, obesity, HIV

**Protocol:**
- Dose: 500µg–5mg methylcobalamin (most bioavailable form)
- Digestive insufficiency: liposomal 1mg
- Retest: short-term (HC + B12); long-term changes in RDW and MCV
- Note: low stomach acidity (antacids, PPIs, SIBO, hypochlorhydria) impairs absorption
- Food sources: clams, beef liver, octopus, oysters

### Folate / B9
- **RBC folate conventional:** 635–3408 nmol/L | **Optimal:** 1,133–3408 nmol/L
- **Serum folate conventional:** 12.46–61.18 nmol/L | **Optimal:** 33.99–61.18 nmol/L
- Essential for WBC/RBC formation, DNA synthesis, cell division, methylation, brain development
- Coenzyme in methylation reactions; alongside B12 for DNA synthesis
- Absorption: ~20% from diet; ~80% from gut microbiota production
- MTHFR variants may require higher folate intake

**Deficiency pattern:** ↑ MCV + ↑ MCH + ↑ RDW + ↑ LDH + ↓ Hb + ↓ HCT + ↓ RBC + ↓ WBC + ↓ neutrophils + ↑ homocysteine

**RBC folate** reflects long-term tissue stores (preferred over serum for chronic assessment); serum folate reflects recent intake only

**Deficiency causes:** poor diet (low raw fruit/vegetables), chronic alcoholism, pregnancy (increased demand), small intestine damage, celiac, IBD, malabsorption, B12 deficiency (blocks cellular folate uptake)
**Neural tube defects:** low maternal folate → fetal neural tube defects; additional 100–400µg DFE recommended

**Key cofactors:**
- Zinc: required for zinc-dependent conjugase enzymes for folate absorption
- B12: deficiency reduces folate cellular absorption and retention

**Increasing folate:** chickpeas, lentils, chicken liver, spinach

**Drugs that decrease folate:** methotrexate, anticonvulsants (phenytoin, phenobarbital), metformin, omeprazole, oral contraceptives, tetracyclines, penicillin derivatives, estrogens

---

### Homocysteine
- **Conventional:** 0–10.3 µmol/L | **Optimal:** 5.0–7.2 µmol/L
- Amino acid formed during incomplete methionine metabolism; reflects functional status of folate, B12, and B6
- **Mechanism:** B12 deficiency → folate can't recycle homocysteine back to methionine → Hcy builds up
- Hcy damages endothelial lining of arteries → atherosclerosis → ↑ CVD and stroke risk
- Each 5 µmol/L ↑ in Hcy = ~20% ↑ CHD risk

**Risk thresholds (updated):**
- Risk begins at ≥9 µmol/L
- Cognitive decline seen at >11 µmol/L; ↑ 88% risk at ≥20 µmol/L
- Arterial stiffness: highest quartile ≥12.80 µmol/L
- CVD: 2–3× ↑ hypertension risk in highest quintile

**High homocysteine associated with:** CVD, arterial disease, stroke, venous thrombosis, Alzheimer's, colon/cervical cancer, depression, IBD, telomere shortening, ↑ CRP, ↑ IL-6
- Vegans/vegetarians: notoriously high (insufficient B12 pushes burden to choline and betaine)

**Low homocysteine (<4 µmol/L):** impairs cysteine/glutathione synthesis → ↓ phase 2 detoxification → ↑ oxidative stress; linked to peripheral neuropathy (41% of idiopathic cases in one large retrospective review)

**Cysteine (from Hcy transsulfuration) is essential for:** glutathione synthesis, taurine production, sulfate production

**Testing companions:** B12 (serum + active), folate (serum + RBC), B6, MMA, vitamin D

---

### Vitamin D (25-OH)
- **Conventional:** 74.88–249.60 nmol/L | **Optimal:** 124.80–224.64 nmol/L
- Best assessed via 25-hydroxy vitamin D (25-OH D) test; do NOT use 1,25-dihydroxy for deficiency screening
- **Functions:** calcium absorption, bone health, blood pressure regulation, immune function, muscle function, cell growth
- Magnesium deficiency impairs vitamin D metabolism

**Updated research thresholds:**
- >75 nmol/L: minimum sufficiency
- ≥100 nmol/L: associated with ↓ all-cause mortality
- ≥137 nmol/L: cancer risk reduction target
- >75 nmol/L: ↓ COVID-19 severity/mortality
- Toxicity risk: >750 nmol/L (monitor serum Ca as levels rise)

**Rule of thumb:** every 1,000 IU D3 raises serum 25-OH D by ~25 nmol/L

**Deficiency treatment (Endocrine Society):**
- 50,000 IU/week × 8 weeks then 1,500–2,000 IU/day maintenance
- Obesity/malabsorption: 6,000–10,000 IU/day until goal, then 3,000–6,000 IU/day maintenance

**Skin pigmentation:** darkest skin types require up to 10× longer sun exposure to synthesise equivalent vitamin D as lightest skin types

**Performance benefits:** ↑ muscle strength (ballet dancers: 18% ↑ isometric strength), improved injury recovery, vertical jump, sprint times, muscle protein synthesis

**Immune:** stimulates expression of antimicrobial peptides in neutrophils, monocytes, NK cells, respiratory epithelium

**Deficiency symptoms:** fatigue, bone/back pain, depression, impaired wound healing, bone loss, hair loss, muscle pain, ↑ infection susceptibility, mood changes, ↓ cognitive function

**Homocysteine connection:** vitamin D activates cystathionine synthase gene → supports Hcy breakdown; high-dose D3 supplementation in deficient women ↓ Hcy, CRP, AST, ALT

**Low vitamin D associated with:** diabetes, cancer, hypertension, CVD, autoimmune/inflammatory disorders, chronic musculoskeletal pain, Hashimoto's

### Zinc
- **Serum conventional:** 7.64–19.88 µmol/L | **Optimal:** 15.14–19.88 µmol/L
- **RBC conventional:** 137.62–224.78 µmol/L | **Optimal:** 159.03–224.78 µmol/L
- **Plasma zinc is the best single marker** (more specific than serum for exchangeable zinc pool); urine zinc declines more rapidly during deficiency
- Cofactor for 300+ enzymes (more than all other trace minerals combined) — including ALP, carbonic anhydrase, SOD, alcohol dehydrogenase, glutathione peroxidase, catalase
- Expect ~95% of clients to be deficient; richest sources (oysters, red meat) are underpromoted

**Functions:** protein synthesis, tissue growth/repair, immune function (macrophages, neutrophils, B/T lymphocytes, NK cells), DNA replication, skin integrity, bone formation, night vision (retinol→retinal), insulin activity, delta-6 desaturase (omega-3/6 conversion), histamine metabolism/breakdown

**Zinc-copper inverse relationship:** as zinc goes up, copper goes down. Excess zinc (>50mg/day) can induce copper deficiency via intestinal metallothionein chelation. Taking 300mg/day zinc will cause immune dysfunction via copper deficiency.

**Hypochlorhydria link:** zinc is a cofactor for carbonic anhydrase — deficiency → ↓ enzyme activity → ↓ HCl production

**Low zinc pattern:** ↓ zinc + ↓ ALP + indicators of hypochlorhydria
**Deficiency symptoms:** acne, poor taste/smell, frequent colds, diarrhea, delayed wound healing, weak immunity, skin rash, ulcers, vision problems, hair loss, depression, sexual dysfunction, sleep disturbances, poor blood sugar regulation

**Non-deficiency causes of low plasma zinc:** inflammation/oxidative stress, ovulatory/luteal phase of cycle, pregnancy, oral contraceptives, adrenal stress

**High zinc:** copper deficiency (excess supplementation), oral contraceptives (↑ RBC zinc), environmental/occupational exposure

**Elevated Cu:Zn ratio >2:** inflammation, advanced disease, ↑ CVD risk, malignancy, reduced recovery
**Children with high Cu:Zn ratio:** associated with aggressive/violent behavioural disorders (88% reduction in disruptive behaviour with nutrient therapy including zinc + B6 + Mg)

**Repletion protocol:**
- Test copper and ceruloplasmin first
- If both zinc and copper are low: use zinc/copper 15:1–20:1 ratio
- Starting dose: 20–60mg/day (RDI 8–14mg; UL 35–40mg based on copper effects)
- Take with food (nausea risk on empty stomach)
- Recheck at 8–12 weeks
- Consider quercetin as zinc ionophore (↑ cellular uptake)
- Add betaine HCl if needed (need HCl to absorb zinc, but zinc is needed to produce HCl)

### Copper
- **Serum conventional:** 10.99–27.48 µmol/L | **Optimal:** 14.13–23.55 µmol/L
- **RBC conventional/optimal:** 8.34–14.32 µmol/L
- Essential trace mineral; highest concentrations in brain and liver
- **Functions:** energy production, iron transport (ceruloplasmin oxidizes Fe²⁺→Fe³⁺ for transferrin loading), neurotransmitter synthesis, antioxidant activity (Cu-Zn SOD), RBC/WBC maturation, bone strength, brain/myelin development, melanin/connective tissue formation, histamine breakdown, antimicrobial phagocytosis

**Copper-iron relationship:** ceruloplasmin loads iron onto transferrin for transport. Copper deficiency = can't load iron for transport → iron deficiency anemia despite adequate iron intake. If seeing iron deficiency on an animal-based diet with good iron intake → suspect copper deficiency first.

**Low copper causes:** copper deficiency (impairs all copper-dependent pathways), excess zinc supplementation, anemia, bone abnormalities, neutropenia, malabsorption, low serum proteins
- Copper deficiency anemia: treated with ~7mg copper/day (vs standard RDI 1mg/day)

**High copper causes:** acute toxicity (copper pipes, contamination), cardiovascular disease association, inflammation (↑ ceruloplasmin as acute-phase reactant), Wilson's disease (genetic copper accumulation in liver/brain/tissues), excessive supplementation, oxidative stress (↑ free copper generates ROS)
- High free copper symptoms: anxiety, panic attacks, inner tension, despair, depression, mood swings, hyperactivity, skin tags, estrogen dominance, emotional meltdowns, tinnitus, abnormal periods, PMS
- The pill increases copper uptake
- High copper → ↑ estrogen dominance + ↑ histamine release

**Intervention for high copper:** zinc supplementation (chelation in GI), vitamin C, molybdenum (molybdenum deficiency predisposes to sulfur sensitivities — high responders to garlic, wine, eggs, smoked salmon), glutamine, histidine, threonine

**Dietary zinc-to-copper ratio:** aim for zinc not more than 15× dietary copper, and not less than 2× dietary copper
**Serum Cu:Zn ratio:**elevated ratio = inflammation marker; more about disease response than nutrition directly (infection mobilises copper from liver to assist immune function, lowering zinc)

---

### Ceruloplasmin
- **Conventional:** 1.42–2.91 µmol/L | **Optimal:** 1.34–1.57 µmol/L
- Copper-containing protein produced mainly by the liver; carries up to 95% of circulating copper
- **Most sensitive indicator of copper deficiency**
- Functions as an oxidase enzyme in iron, copper, and glutathione metabolism; also has nitric oxide (NO) activity

**Low ceruloplasmin:** copper deficiency (confirm with ↓ serum Cu + ↓ RBC Cu), anemia (↓ Fe transport), hepatic insufficiency/fibrosis, Menkes disease (genetic — ↓ ceruloplasmin + ↓ Cu + ↓ urine Cu), Wilson's disease (↓/normal ceruloplasmin despite ↑ tissue copper + ↑↑ urine Cu), excess zinc supplementation

**High ceruloplasmin:** copper toxicity (↑ ceruloplasmin + ↑ RBC Cu + ↑ serum Cu + ↑ urine Cu), acute-phase reactant (infection, inflammation, CVD, cancer — correlates with hs-CRP), rheumatoid arthritis (protective role), primary biliary cirrhosis
- ↑ ceruloplasmin = independent risk factor for cardiac events; more common in older females with CVD risk factors

**Testing recommendation:** assess alongside serum copper, RBC copper, hs-CRP, ferritin, homocysteine, fibrinogen, IL-6, neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio

**Aceruloplasminemia:** genetic absence of ceruloplasmin → iron accumulates in pancreas → can lead to diabetes

**Falsely decreased:** estrogen therapy, oral contraceptive use, excess zinc supplementation

### Sodium (Na)
- **Conventional range:** ~135–145 mmol/L
- **Optimal:** similar — rarely falls outside range unless severe
- Most predominant mineral lost through sweat (~80–90% of sweat composition)
- **Functions:** nerve signals, muscle contractions, kidney & heart health, adrenal function, fluid balance
- **Always read alongside potassium** (like two contacts — don't read one without the other)

**Low sodium (hyponatremia, <120):** renal disease, congestive heart failure, Addison's disease, vomiting/diarrhea, burns  
**High sodium:** dehydration, Cushing's, excess sodium intake  
**Practical pattern:**  
- High sodium + low potassium → adrenal stress load  
- Low sodium + high potassium → adrenal insufficiency  
- Either pattern = acute or chronic stress response  
**Functional clue:** even borderline low sodium can indicate how someone's body is handling stress, even if they don't feel stressed. Watch for fatigue, dizziness, brain fog, salt cravings.

---

### Potassium (K)
- **Functions:** muscle function, fluid balance, nerve signalling, blood pressure regulation
- One of the most important minerals for performance clients
- Kidneys tightly regulate it — unlike B12, it cannot be stored long-term; deficiency can develop relatively quickly
- **Recommendation:** 1L coconut water/day provides ~1–2g potassium; add Celtic sea salt (¼–½ tsp per litre)

**Low potassium symptoms:** chronic fatigue (especially exercise-related), muscle cramps, heart palpitations, lightheadedness, high blood pressure, rhabdomyolysis risk  
**High potassium:** can be linked to dehydration  
**For weight-cut athletes:** tightly monitor and regulate sodium and potassium  
**If insufficient dietary intake:** consider potassium citrate (most electrolyte supplements are too low in potassium)

---

### Chloride
- **Functions:** required to make hydrochloric acid (HCl) in the stomach
- **Low chloride:** vomiting/diarrhea, Cushing's, hypochlorhydria (low stomach acid)
- Not a standalone diagnostic — it's a clue. Low chloride may indicate possible gut issues (low stomach acid / hypochlorhydria) or cortisol/hormonal dysfunction
- Useful when seen alongside other GI or adrenal markers

---

### Bicarbonate / CO2
- On blood test: may be labelled "bicarbonate" or "CO2" — they are the same thing
- **Functions:** acid-base buffering agent (buffers acidity and lactate buildup)
- Athletes may supplement sodium bicarbonate to extend time to fatigue during high-intensity exercise

**Low bicarbonate pattern:**
- May indicate B1 (thiamine) deficiency → pyruvate can't enter Krebs cycle → excess lactate production → acid buildup consumes bicarbonate
- Symptoms: fatigue, "burnt out" feeling even at low training loads, poor exercise tolerance
- Also seen with lactic acidosis, impaired energy metabolism

**Gut connection:**
- When HCl decreases in the stomach → hydrogen ions increase → bicarbonate rises as compensation
- Pattern: **elevated bicarbonate + decreased chloride** = possible hypochlorhydria / gut acid issues

**Anion Gap (combines electrolytes):**
- High anion gap: linked to metabolic acidosis, impaired immune function (leukocyte function), altered oxygen-Hb binding (fatigue), arrhythmias, hypertension
- Functional sign: deep or rapid breathing at rest may indicate body trying to expel CO2 to compensate for acidosis
- Supportive nutraceuticals: CoQ10/ubiquinol, B vitamins (especially B1), magnesium

---

### BUN — Blood Urea Nitrogen (Urea)
- **What it is:** waste product of protein metabolism; nitrogen from amino acid breakdown is excreted as urea
- **Functions:** marker of protein catabolism and kidney filtration
- **High BUN:** high protein diet, dehydration, excessive muscle breakdown, high training load
- **Low BUN:** severe liver damage (cirrhosis) — liver can't process nitrogen
- **Important in athletes:** high protein intake + heavy training can elevate BUN — does NOT automatically indicate kidney dysfunction
- Mnemonic: "protein to grow your buns" = BUN + protein

---

### eGFR — Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate
- **What it measures:** how well kidneys are filtering blood (removing waste)
- **Target:** above 90 mL/min/1.73m² (Australian labs often just report ">90")
- Normal to decline with age; females naturally have lower values than males

**Stages:**
- >90: normal
- 60–90: early stage — investigate
- 15–60: moderate-severe chronic kidney disease
- <15: kidney failure / emergency

**Limitations:**
- Significantly influenced by muscle mass — high muscle mass athletes will show artificially elevated creatinine and potentially altered eGFR
- Creatinine and eGFR are late-stage markers — do not catch early kidney dysfunction
- A doctor may flag high creatinine in a muscular client and incorrectly advise stopping creatine supplementation — always consider training status and protein intake as confounders

**Steroids and kidney function:**
- Any client on anabolic compounds should monitor eGFR and especially Cystatin C
- Steroids are associated with progressive kidney damage; many deaths in bodybuilding/strongman may have been preventable with better early testing

---

### Cystatin C
- **Gold standard marker for kidney function** — superior to eGFR/creatinine
- NOT influenced by muscle mass (unlike creatinine) → more accurate in athletes
- Early marker: can catch kidney dysfunction while eGFR and creatinine are still "normal"
- **High Cystatin C = poor kidney function**
- **Low Cystatin C = good kidney function**
- Inverse relationship with eGFR
- **Mandatory for:** any client on steroids, SARMs, or aggressive training/supplement protocols
- More sensitive — detects issues earlier than creatinine + eGFR combination
- Must request specifically from doctor (not always included in standard panels)

---

### Creatinine (Kidney Marker)
- **Note:** creatinine (the blood marker) ≠ creatine (the supplement) — completely different
- Byproduct of muscle creatine phosphate breakdown
- **High in athletes:** normal — reflects high muscle mass and protein turnover, NOT kidney damage
- High creatinine + high muscle mass + high protein intake = expected finding, not pathology
- Doctors may flag and advise stopping creatine supplementation unnecessarily — context matters enormously
- Use Cystatin C for confirmation when creatinine is ambiguous in muscular clients

---

For clients with early kidney dysfunction signals, elevated creatinine (non-muscle related), or steroid use:

| Compound | Dose | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|
| Black seed oil | 1–2g/day | First-line; broad preventative + kidney protective |
| Astragalus | 3–10g/day for 3–12 months | Slows CKD progression; reduces proteinuria |
| Terminalia | 2,000mg/day | Comparable to drug therapy for uric acid + kidney markers; also supports liver |
| Rhubarb extract | 350mg | Kidney protective |
| Grape seed extract | Standard dose | Antimicrobial (gut) + can improve eGFR over 6 months |

**Key note:** Herbs and plants often require months of consistent use to show effect. Trial 1–2 at a time.  
**Blood pressure** is one of the most significant risk factors for kidney disease — even 127–131/80–85 is a risk factor.

## ENZYMES — PANCREATIC & INFLAMMATORY MARKERS

### Lipase
- **Conventional:** 13–60 U/L | **Optimal:** 22–51 U/L
- Enzyme essential for fat digestion; produced mainly by the pancreas
- Breaks down triglycerides into glycerol and fatty acids (with aid of bile salts)
- In acute pancreatic injury, both lipase and amylase are released into the bloodstream
- Lipase remains elevated longer than amylase after acute pancreatitis → more useful for monitoring

**↓ Lipase (Pancreatic Insufficiency):**
- Suggests reduced pancreatic enzyme output / sluggish pancreatic function
- Commonly associated: ↑ amylase, ↓ WBC, ↓ BUN, ↓ albumin, ↑ globulin, ↑ GGT
- Often occurs alongside hypochlorhydria (low stomach acid is a significant contributing factor)

**↑ Lipase (Pancreatic Inflammation):**
- Greater elevation above optimal → higher likelihood of existing or developing pancreatic disorder

**↑ Lipase (Gallbladder Dysfunction):**
- Biliary stasis, early-stage cholecystitis, overt gallbladder obstruction
- Supporting findings: ↑ GGT, ↑ ALP, normal or ↑ AST/ALT, ↑ total and direct bilirubin (raises suspicion for biliary obstruction with possible gallstones)

---

### Amylase
- **Conventional:** 21–103 U/L | **Optimal:** 40–86 U/L
- Enzyme that breaks down starch into sugar; produced by salivary glands and pancreas
- In acute pancreatitis, amylase rises sharply within 3–6 hours of onset — key early diagnostic indicator

**↓ Amylase (Pancreatic Insufficiency):**
- Suggests reduced pancreatic enzyme output
- Commonly associated: ↓ WBC, ↓ BUN, ↓ lipase, ↓ albumin, ↑ globulin, ↑ GGT
- Often occurs alongside hypochlorhydria

**↑ Amylase (Pancreatic Inflammation):**
- Degree of elevation above optimal correlates with likelihood of pancreatitis

**↑ Amylase also seen in:** alcoholic patients, pregnant women, diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)

---

### CRP / hs-CRP (C-Reactive Protein)
- **Male conventional:** 0–1 mg/L | **Male optimal:** 0–0.55 mg/L
- **Female conventional:** 0–1 mg/L | **Female optimal:** 0–1 mg/L
- Acute inflammatory marker produced by the liver (in response to IL-6)
- Strong predictor of CHD risk — may be a stronger predictor of heart attack/stroke than high cholesterol

**Confounding influencers:** hormone therapy, pregnancy, birth control pills, chronic inflammation (arthritis)

**Common causes of ↑ hs-CRP:**
- Diet high in saturated fat and high-glycemic carbohydrates
- Periodontal disease (can almost double heart attack risk; CRP drops significantly after periodontal therapy)
- Smoking
- High blood pressure (chronic vascular inflammation)
- Abdominal obesity, insulin resistance

**Falsely decreased:** fibrates, niacin, statins, NSAIDs, lipemia, moderate alcohol consumption, weight loss

**Falsely increased:** cigarette smoking, estrogen/progesterone use, Heinz body formation (G6PDD, chronic liver disease, DKA, alpha-thalassemia, asplenia/hypersplenism, lymphoma), heterophilic antibodies (e.g. rheumatoid factor), hyperglobulinemia, monoclonal gammopathy, recent injury or illness

---

### LDH (Lactate Dehydrogenase)
- **Conventional:** 100–200 IU/L | **Optimal:** 140–200 IU/L
- Group of enzymes involved in carbohydrate metabolism — specifically conversion of pyruvate to lactate in the presence of NAD/NADH
- Present in high concentrations in heart, liver, kidneys, skeletal muscle, brain, RBCs, lungs
- **Total LDH alone has limited diagnostic value** — if significantly elevated, perform LDH isoenzyme study to identify tissue of origin
- LDH is one of the **key indirect clues for thiamine (B1) deficiency** (alongside anion gap)

**Lactate recycling (Cori cycle):** lactate enters bloodstream from muscles/RBCs → liver converts back to glucose → returned to blood

**↓ LDH:**
- Linked to hypoglycemia (especially reactive hypoglycemia), pancreatic dysfunction, impaired glucose metabolism
- Often presents with ↓ fasting BGL, ↓ HbA1c

**↑ LDH — causes:**

| Pattern | Key markers |
|---------|------------|
| Liver/biliary dysfunction | ↑ total/direct bilirubin, ↑ AST, ↑ GGT, ↑ ALP — rule out hepatitis, EBV, CMV |
| CVD / acute MI | Elevated in ~90–95% of acute MI; rises 24–48h post-MI; AST rises 4–10× reference, peaks at 24h, normalises in 3–4 days |
| Megaloblastic anaemia (B12/folate) | Elevated in ~85% of cases; LDH-1 isoenzyme elevated; ↑ MCH, ↑ MCV, ↑ RDW, ↑ serum iron, ↓ RBC, ↓ Hgb |
| Haemolytic anaemia | ↑ LDH common; ~60% show LDH-1/LDH-2 reversal; normal LDH makes haemolytic anaemia less likely |
| Non-specific tissue inflammation | ↑ basophils (>3), ↑ ESR, ↓ albumin, ↓ total protein, ↓ globulin |
| Viral infection | ↑ in ~95% of EBV/infectious mononucleosis; also CMV; ↓ WBC week 1, ↑ WBC week 2; ↑ ALP/AST 5–14 days; ↑ GGT 7–21 days |
| Tissue destruction | Any tissue damage → cell cytoplasm rupture → LDH release; if >10 U/L above normal, perform isoenzyme fractionation |

**Falsely decreased:** certain medications
**Falsely increased:** haemolysis during blood draw, strenuous exercise, skin diseases, children <18 years, pregnancy

## IGF-1 (Insulin-Like Growth Factor 1)

- Also called somatomedin C
- Hormone and protein that works alongside growth hormone (GH) to support normal growth of bone, muscle, and other tissues
- Unlike GH (which fluctuates throughout the day), IGF-1 is relatively stable → reliable marker for GH activity
- Concentrations increase throughout childhood, peak at puberty, then progressively decline in adulthood

**Age-specific reference ranges (QUEST Diagnostics):**

| Age | Range (ng/mL) |
|-----|--------------|
| 18–19.9 yrs | 108–548 |
| 20–24.9 yrs | 83–456 |
| 25–29.9 yrs | 63–373 |
| 30–39.9 yrs | 53–331 |
| 40–49.9 yrs | 52–328 |
| 50–59.9 yrs | 50–317 |
| 60–69.9 yrs | 41–279 |
| 70–79.9 yrs | 34–245 |
| 80+ yrs | 34–246 |

**Regulation:** nutrient intake (calories, protein, carbohydrates) and insulin signalling central to IGF-1 production. GH stimulates IGF-1 mainly in the liver. Leucine/amino acid intake strongly regulates IGF-1 and mTOR. Insulin itself can stimulate IGF-1 when elevated.

**mTOR link:** IGF-1 directly stimulates mTOR — this ties growth hormone, nutrient intake, and cellular energy availability into a unified anabolic pathway. Like mTOR, IGF-1 follows a U-shaped risk curve: too little = frailty/wasting; chronic overactivation = accelerated aging + cancer risk.

**↓ IGF-1 associations:**
- Metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, dyslipidaemia, obesity, cardiovascular disease
- Bone density loss, osteoporosis risk
- Reduced lean mass and muscular strength
- Persistent fatigue, ↓ exercise tolerance
- Cognitive decline, dementia/Alzheimer's risk (higher IGF-1 correlates with larger brain volume)
- Caloric restriction: at 30–40% of normal energy, IGF-1 drops 40–60 ng/mL; at 70–80% restriction, minimal drop
- Growth hormone resistance (chronic illness, malnutrition, hypothyroidism, sex hormone deficiency)
- Pituitary dysfunction (↓ GH output → ↓ IGF-1)

**↑ IGF-1 associations:**
- Hyperinsulinaemia / obesity / high refined carbohydrate diet
- Pituitary adenoma (GH-secreting tumour — most common cause of persistent ↑ IGF-1; confirm with glucose suppression test)
- Acromegaly (adults — abnormal skeletal/organ growth, ↑ T2D, hypertension, arthritis, ↑ CVD risk, reduced lifespan)
- Gigantism (children — excessive GH before growth plates close)
- Increased cancer risk (UK Biobank, n=500,000): breast cancer HR ~1.11/+5 nmol/L; prostate cancer HR ~1.09/+5 nmol/L; colorectal cancer HR ~1.11/+1 SD — but risk data are from middle-aged mixed-lifestyle populations; younger athletes with high IGF-1 reflect growth/training, not pathological pathway activation

**Clinical context note:** Always interpret against age-specific ranges. A 25-year-old athlete with high-normal IGF-1 is a different risk profile to a 55-year-old sedentary individual. Monitor trend over time in young athletes.

## NUTRACEUTICAL PROTOCOLS — NUTRITIONAL SUPPORT

### Thiamine (B1) Protocol

**Key functions:**
- Converts pyruvate → acetyl CoA → Krebs cycle (energy production)
- Required for ATP synthesis from carbohydrates
- Nerve function / neurotransmitter synthesis (mood, energy, focus)
- Gut health — building block of stomach acid; supports gastric motility
- Lactate clearance (reduces muscle burning during exercise)
- Heart function and circulation
- Antioxidant/anti-inflammatory protection

**High-risk groups:** diabetics (76%), obese post-bariatric (49%), elderly (40–48%), cancer (55%), ER patients (20%), CHF (33%), pregnant/hyperemesis (38%), psychiatric patients (30%)

**High-carb diet depletes B1:** when ≥55% of calories come from carbohydrates (regardless of source), thiamine status declines in otherwise healthy individuals. Any client on 300–400g+ carbs/day or in a caloric surplus should likely supplement B1.

**Red flags in client screening:**
- High carb intake / caloric surplus
- Alcohol consumption
- Gut issues (bloating, constipation, nausea, GERD)
- Stress
- Medications: metformin, PPIs, diuretics
- Gut malabsorption (SIBO, IBD, Crohn's, celiac, hypochlorhydria)

**Client presentation:** heavy fatigue despite good sleep, weakness in gym, irritability, tired after meals, anxiety, constipation, rapid heartbeat, PMS, poor focus, brain fog, difficulty losing fat, muscle burning

**Indirect blood markers for B1 deficiency (not definitive — use with symptoms):**
- ↓ LDH (underactive pyruvate conversion enzyme)
- ↑ Anion gap (excess acids)
- ↓ CO2/bicarbonate
- ↑ glucose (poor carbohydrate processing)

**Supplement selection:**

| Form | Notes | Dose |
|------|-------|------|
| Thiamine hydrochloride | Water soluble; least strong; good starting point; lowest paradoxical reaction risk | 500mg–4,000mg |
| Benfotiamine | Fat-soluble; better cell/brain penetration; **use when GGT >20** | 300–1,000mg |
| TTFD | Fat-soluble; strong; **only use when GGT is low/normal** (depletes glutathione) | 100–800mg |
| Thiamiga | 4 fat-soluble forms; mega-dose option | High-dose protocol |

**GGT guidance:** if GGT >20 → benfotiamine; if GGT low/normal → TTFD. Do not use TTFD when GGT is already elevated (glutathione depletion risk).

**Co-factors required:** always pair with magnesium (6–11mg/kg/day split) + B complex. B1 supplementation increases demand for all energy metabolism nutrients: electrolytes, minerals, B vitamins, ALA, inositol, choline, amino acids (taurine, cysteine, tryptophan, glutamate), BCAAs.

**Dosing protocol:**
- Start low: 100mg once daily
- Titrate up in 50–100mg steps once stable
- Split doses throughout the day (absorption is water-soluble, not time-critical)
- Can take with or without food (but strong taste — food recommended)
- No UL for B1 (water soluble, excreted in urine)

**Therapeutic ranges (clinical use):** 100–400mg typical; some with genetic impairments may need 500–2,000mg/day

### Magnesium Protocol

**Dosing:** 7–10mg/kg body weight/day to reach optimal levels (e.g. 80kg = 560–800mg/day)
- Up to 10mg/kg if outputs are high (heavy training, sauna, heat stress)
- Split doses to avoid loose stools

**Factors that increase need:**
- High physical/cognitive output
- Degree of deficiency
- Body composition
- Gut absorption issues (low retention even with moderate supplementation at 400mg)
- High sweat rate / heat exposure
- Alcohol consumption
- PPIs or antacids
- Stress and anxiety

**Case study approach:**
- Mild-moderate deficiency (serum 0.7–0.8) already on 300–400mg/day → add 3mg/kg on top of current intake
- Severe deficiency (<0.7) already at RDI → add 4–6mg/kg
- If Mg intake <100mg/day → add 7–10mg/kg
- Address gut absorption issues concurrently
- Retest in 8–12 weeks

**Serum magnesium note:** represents only 1–5% of total body magnesium stores (most in bone, RBCs, organ tissues). Many test within normal range but remain subclinically deficient. If serum magnesium is low → likely severely deficient. Serum Mg: conventional low = 0.7, optimal target = 0.9.

**Forms:**
- Preferred: Mg blends (Fusion Magnesium Advanced, Source Naturals Ultra-Mag, Ethical Nutrients Magnesium with Taurine)
- Bioavailable forms: citrate, glycinate, taurate, aspartate
- Avoid: Mg oxide (unless intentionally stimulating bowel movement)

**Key interactions:**
- Cofactor for B1 activation — must pair with magnesium when supplementing thiamine
- Required for proper calcium and vitamin D metabolism (inadequate Mg → impaired utilisation of Ca and D)
- Activates GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter) → supports mood, sleep, anxiety reduction
- Strongly linked to depression: 125–300mg glycinate/taurate with each meal → significant depression reversal in studies
- 86% reduction in muscle cramps reported in swimmers supplementing magnesium

### Copper Protocol

**Assessment checklist:**
- Dietary intake: <8mg/day from whole foods? Red meat <2 servings/week?
- Absorption issues: history of hypochlorhydria, frequent diarrhoea, malabsorption, cancer/chemotherapy (especially adolescence)
- Blood markers: iron deficiency anaemia, vitamin D deficiency, euthyroid with cold nodules

**Symptoms of deficiency:** fatigue, feeling cold, irritability, shortness of breath, heavy bleed

**Dietary:** ↑ beef liver (150g/week)

**Sample protocol:**
- Month 1: Zinc 16mg + B1 100mg (if zinc status unknown)
- Month 2: Zinc 42mg (once zinc confirmed low, e.g. bottom quintile)
- Iron bisglycinate: 48mg/day × 8 weeks → retest → reduce to 24mg if transferrin saturation >10%; if not >10%, maintain + address gut absorption

**Dosage:**
- AI: 1.7mg (M) / 1.2mg (F) per day
- UL: 10mg/day (no evidence of adverse effects at 10–12mg/day from foods in normal copper homeostasis)

**Gut support:** 1 tsp ACV pre-meal

**Copper anemia blood associations:**
- ↓ HCT (<37% women / <40% men)
- ↓ HGB (<13.5 g/dL women / <14 g/dL men) and ↓ RBCs (<3.9 women / <4.2 men)
- ↑ MCV (>89.9) — low or high
- ↑ or ↓ MCH (>31.9) — increased or decreased hair copper alongside → copper anemia possible

**Copper, ceruloplasmin and iron triangle:**
- Ceruloplasmin loads iron onto transferrin (FOX enzyme requires copper + vitamin A + magnesium)
- Copper deficiency → can't load iron for transport → apparent iron deficiency despite adequate iron intake
- Animal-based diet with iron deficiency → more likely copper deficiency than actual iron deficiency

**High copper + low zinc → anxiety:**
- Mechanism: zinc regulates GABA/glutamate balance; zinc deficiency → GABAergic impairment → anxiety, racing thoughts, irritability, poor sleep
- Protocol: zinc picolinate + vitamin C + vitamin E + B6 + magnesium × 8 weeks → anxiety scores ~30% improvement

### B12 Protocol

**Dosage:** 500mcg–5mg (no UL); most bioavailable form = methylcobalamin

**Digestive insufficiency:** go liposomal 1mg
**Vegan/plant-based:** require more aggressive support

**Absorption requirements:**
- Needs intrinsic factor (produced in stomach)
- Low stomach acidity (antacids, PPIs, SIBO, hypochlorhydria) impairs absorption
- Best dietary sources: clams, beef liver, octopus, oysters

**Retest:**
- Short-term: homocysteine and active B12 (holoTC)
- Long-term: changes in RDW and MCV

**Parietal cell antibodies / AITD connection:**
- AITD (autoimmune thyroid disease) occurs in ~40% of pernicious anaemia (PA) patients
- 24.7% of AITD patients are positive for parietal cell antibodies (PCA)
- 24% of AITD patients with PCA (but asymptomatic) developed overt autoimmune gastritis (AIG) on biopsy
- 53% of AIG patients had an associated thyroid disorder — 75.7% autoimmune in nature
- **Clinical implication:** when seeing AITD/Hashimoto's, screen for B12 deficiency and parietal cell antibodies; and vice versa

### Zinc Protocol

**Always test copper and ceruloplasmin first before prescribing zinc**

**If both zinc and copper are low:** use zinc/copper combination at 15:1–20:1 ratio

**Starting dose:** 20–60mg/day
- RDI: 8–14mg/day
- UL: 35–40mg/day (based on adverse effects on copper status)
- LOAEL: 60mg/day (lowest observed adverse effect level)
- Take with food (nausea risk on empty stomach)

**Recheck:** 8–12 weeks; if replete, reassess whether to sustain, reduce, or remove

**Forms:**
- Preferred: blends (Doctor's Best PepZin GI Zinc-L-Carnosine, Seeking Health Zinc Carnosine)
- Bioavailable: citrate, gluconate, L-carnosine
- Avoid: oxide

**Bioavailability data:** zinc citrate 61.3%, zinc gluconate 60.9%, zinc oxide 49.9%

**Absorption inhibitors:** phytates (beans, wheat, corn, nuts), calcium supplements, competes with copper and iron absorption

**UL rationale:** LOAEL 60mg ÷ uncertainty factor 1.5 = UL 40mg/day; reduced copper is the primary concern, not direct toxicity

**Zinc and stomach acid (carbonic anhydrase):**
- Zinc is a cofactor for carbonic anhydrase → catalyses CO2 + H2O → bicarbonate + protons → HCl production
- Zinc deficiency → ↓ carbonic anhydrase activity → ↓ HCl → hypochlorhydria
- Can combine with Betaine HCl protocol (need HCl to absorb zinc, but need zinc to make stomach acid — start one then add other)

**Quercetin:** consider as zinc ionophore to increase cellular zinc uptake

## NOTES FOR AI ANALYSIS

When analysing a client's blood results:
1. **Flag values outside optimal ranges** (not just conventional ranges)
2. **Look for PATTERNS across multiple markers** — single markers are rarely diagnostic
3. **Always consider dehydration first** before diagnosing elevated haematocrit/Hb
4. **Consider female cycle timing** when interpreting ferritin
5. **Note medications** that can falsely alter results
6. **Suggest follow-up testing** where appropriate (e.g., methylmalonic acid for B12, OGTT for glucose)
7. **Maintain ethical framing** — educational interpretation only, always recommend GP referral for concerning patterns
8. **Do not diagnose** — identify patterns and potential links, suggest further investigation
